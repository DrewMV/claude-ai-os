Product and Solution Vision

People at work are thirsting for context, yearning to know that what they do contributes to a larger whole.

– Daniel Pink, Drive [1]

Definition: The Solution Vision represents the future state of the solution under development. It serves as a reflection of customer and stakeholder needs and the proposed product or service to meet those needs.

Summary

The purpose of a product or solution vision is to help everyone contributing to understand what the future will look like and how it relates to their work. It reflects the needs of customers and stakeholders, offering a clear view of how the organization intends to solve a business challenge. It sets the creative direction for the organization and motivates people at all levels to work together to realize that future. This clear direction fosters alignment, collaboration, and quick decision-making while allowing ARTs and teams to act with autonomy, creativity, and agility toward common goals.

What is a product or solution vision?

A vision is a clear and motivating description of the future intended to engage team members while setting limits and context for what they are creating. Its mission is to inspire and align everyone in the organization. This helps people see what changes are coming and visualize the impact on their customers. It motivates action, aids decision-making, and helps teams make quick choices based on a shared goal. As a result, it allows individuals and teams to collaborate while allowing them to work independently as needed.

Without a well-crafted vision, misalignment, delays, waste, defects, and reduced engagement are likely to occur. This hampers the organization's ability to deliver value for itself and its customers. Thus, a compelling vision is crucial to avoid poor outcomes.

How is a product or solution vision expressed?

In today's competitive business environment, workforce passion is a critical advantage. Companies with purpose and engagement outperform those that lack these qualities. A motivating vision helps establish a culture where agility can thrive. To move people, a vision must possess two critical characteristics:

Aspirational yet realistic and achievable – A product or solution vision must be compelling and futuristic while practical enough to be accomplished within a meaningful timeframe.
Motivational – The vision must align with the Portfolio's Strategic Themes and inspire the Agile Release Trains (ARTs) and Agile teams.

An exceptional vision aligned with these characteristics ignites the workforce's dedication and creativity to embrace and pursue a compelling picture of the future. When the organization collaboratively works to bring this vision to life, remarkable things can happen.

Effectively conveying the vision requires selecting a format that suits the context and the audience. Different stakeholders may prefer different modes of communication. Employing imaginative approaches such as a postcard from the future (Figure 1), a video, a press release, or an infographic vividly portrays the future-state product or solution from the customer's point of view.

How does a product or solution vision create alignment?

Translating the vision into action requires more than inspiration; it requires practical guidance toward a clear path forward. For teams and ARTs to effectively implement the vision, they need to understand what they are building, who will benefit, and how it will address the customer's pain points. To be credible, a product or solution vision description must have the following attributes:

Desirable – Do customers and users want the product or solution?
Viable – Can we build and offer the product or solution for more value than the cost?
Feasible – Can we deliver the right product or solution through a combination of build, buy, acquire, or partner endeavors?
Sustainable – Are we proactively managing the product or solution to account for its expected lifecycle?

During development, goals, metrics, and the design thinking process will refine the team's understanding of the product or solution through these attributes. This iterative approach provides context to create a more effective business model. The vision must evolve as organizations learn more about customers and their needs.

Purpose

The first element is the purpose, which addresses the 'why' of the product or solution. This requires careful analysis of customers and their needs. The questions in Figure 3 help establish the purpose.

Figure 3. Questions to help establish the product or solution purpose

Once the purpose is understood, the product or solution must be 'positioned' and described so that customers can understand what it is and what it does for them. In 'Crossing the Chasm,' [2] Geoffrey Moore explains the importance of 'product positioning' as a "way to create and occupy a space inside the target customers' heads." He also notes that customers won't allow you to consume much of that space; therefore, positioning statements must be clear and brief. This exercise is a powerful focusing technique to help crystallize the critical purpose element of the solution vision.

Each element is described further below and is followed by an example.

For – A customer-centric approach starts with sharply defining the customer for the product or solution. This should be as focused as possible, assuring that it will meet the needs of a specific, targeted customer or segment. These customers will then inform the personas central to design thinking.
Who – This prompt answers why the identified customer might be interested in a new product or solution. What problem are they facing with the current alternative? What is unique about their situation? What pain are they experiencing now?
Our Product – "Potential customers cannot buy what they cannot name, nor can they seek out the product unless they know what category to look under." [2] To have any meaningful discussion—internally and externally—the product or solution needs a working name, a category, and a description people can relate to when discussing it.
That – This portion of the statement describes in simple terms what the product or solution will do for that customer and how it addresses their pain.
Unlike – This section identifies the features that differentiate this product or solution from the current market alternatives. Focus on differentiating from no more than one or two primary competitive alternatives.

Solution intent

Solution intent is what turns a big idea into a well-defined plan. It explains what the current and future versions of what a product or solution should look like. Then, it outlines the knowledge needed to build and improve the product or solution. Solution intent breaks down the big vision into practical requirements and designs that Agile teams can use for their work. It applies to products and solutions of all sizes and serves as a central place to gather information for everyone involved in creating the solution. Solution intent can be presented as anything from simple drawings on a whiteboard to detailed technical documents.

Solution intent ensures that a product or solution meets the needs of its intended users. That often includes following important rules and requirements, which teams write down instead of talking informally about them. This helps manage what's needed to create the solution properly, including details like specifications and how everything will be put together.

This isn't just for big systems like airplanes or MRI machines. Even when creating smaller initiatives like websites or mobile apps, there are significant design choices to consider. For instance, requirements about cybersecurity, data privacy, how the system works with others, keeping records of events, how well it performs, and staying true to company branding can all be essential to achieving the project's overall goal.

Backlogs

Backlogs are the actionable expression of the vision. The vision and the solution intent inform the work to be done to deliver the desired outcomes. They appear as a dynamic list in team, art, and solution train backlogs. These decentralized backlogs evolve as teams learn more and customer needs reveal themselves. Business Owners, System Architects, Solution Architects, Release Train Engineers, and Solution Train Engineers collaborate with each other and the teams.

Product Owners, Product Management, and Solution Management help their teams and ARTs maintain alignment between their backlogs and the vision. They continuously refine and prioritize the backlogs so the most valuable items are ready to implement. Teams and ARTs work on the higher-priority stories, features, and capabilities in their backlogs. The goal is to launch functionality incrementally and deliver a continuous flow of value for the solution vision.

How do organizations achieve a shared vision?

Everyone in the value stream must contribute to achieving a shared vision. Leaders and team members must consistently align, undeterred by inevitable distractions.

As a result, leaders need to communicate the vision effectively and regularly. While formal events like PI Planning are essential for sharing strategy, it's crucial to use other methods, too. Patrick Lencioni's book, 'The Advantage,' [3] emphasizes various communication channels—town halls, staff meetings, newsletters, and personal conversations—to keep key messages fresh in everyone's mind. By repeatedly sharing the vision, leaders help ensure everyone is on the same page. Leaders must actively listen for signs of confusion and address them quickly.

Equally, achieving a shared vision relies on the commitment and engagement of the workforce. ARTs and teams need to embrace the vision wholeheartedly, channeling their passion and dedication to bring the solution to life. This necessitates the courage and commitment to engage with the vision within the context of the ART and the teams. A clear vision captures hearts and minds. It provides people with the purpose and motivation needed to drive toward the future state. It also offers a clear rationale and a forward path, as outlined in the solution purpose, solution intent, and backlogs. Equipped with this information, teams, ARTs, and leaders have the necessary guidance to deliver desirable, viable, feasible, and sustainable products and solutions. They, in turn, generate continuous value for the organization and its customers.

Key Takeaways
A product or solution vision is a view of the future meant to align and inspire the organization.
A vision should be tailored to the intended audience.
The vision is reflected in the purpose, solution intent, and backlogs.
Keeping the vision in focus during PI execution maintains alignment.
