Portfolio Backlog

Innovation comes from the producer, not the customer.

– W. Edwards Deming, Paraphrased from Out of the Crisis [1]

Definition: The Portfolio Backlog is a Kanban system that is used to capture and manage the business and enabler epics intended to create and evolve the portfolio's products, services, and solutions.

Summary

Portfolio Leadership is responsible for developing, maintaining, and prioritizing the Portfolio backlog. They actively collaborate with all the other stakeholders, to identify the epics needed to advance the portfolio's products and solutions. Portfolio epics are large (and typically cross-cutting initiatives) managed through the portfolio Kanban. Since more effort and capacity are required as the epic travels from the left to the right of the Kanban, Portfolio Leadership carefully decides which will proceed to each subsequent step or be removed altogether. The goal is to determine which portfolio items have the most business value to move on to development.

How is the portfolio backlog built and refined?

The portfolio backlog is essential to Lean Portfolio Management (LPM). Portfolio Leadership, the Value Management Office(VMO), and other key stakeholders across the organization work together to manage this backlog, helping identify and prioritize the most valuable epics for the organization's goals.

To build and refine the backlog, LPM applies a flow-based approach to ensure portfolio epics have the appropriate levels of discovery and risk and are ready for implementation. Backlog refinement activities often occur during the Portfolio Sync and the Strategic Portfolio Review events. Portfolio Leadership and its stakeholders add new backlog items, update priorities, and remove less valuable epics. A well-maintained backlog is essential to manage the portfolio successfully.

Refining the portfolio backlog often involves the following activities:

Reviewing new epics and determining their alignment with the portfolio's strategic themes and vision
Evaluating the epic hypothesis statement to decide whether it warrants assignment to an Epic Owner
Prioritizing the backlog using Weighted Shortest Job First (WSJF) and other factors in collaboration with Business Owners, Enterprise Architects, Product Management, and other stakeholders

How is the portfolio Kanban used to manage the backlog?

The portfolio Kanban system visualizes and facilitates flow through the portfolio backlog. Epics proceed visibly through various states until they are rejected or move to the next state. Epics require analysis and approval from Portfolio Leadership before being committed to implementation. Epic Owners are responsible for bringing insights about their epics to the conversations with Portfolio Leadership. Enterprise Architects play a critical role in the Kanban, typically guiding the enabler epics that support the technical considerations for business epics.

Many companies struggle with innovation and cannot manage portfolios with enough rigor or discipline. Less critical efforts remain active while those with more potential starve. Simply picking amongst the best big ideas will not advance the portfolio from its current state to a better future state. Instead, Portfolio Leadership and its stakeholders must identify which epics will achieve the portfolio's vision.

Although opportunistic concepts are welcome, LPM has a fiduciary responsibility to facilitate epics best aligned with the portfolio's strategic themes, vision, and economic framework. Achieving a better future state requires development value streams to adopt a 'one-portfolio' mindset. This encourages collaboration to achieve the portfolio's higher-level objectives and the organization's broader goals.

The portfolio Kanban helps align strategy and execution by highlighting and governing the selection of the most significant and strategic epics for the portfolio. Portfolio Leadership is responsible for the portfolio Kanban, often reviewing it during the Strategic Portfolio Review and Portfolio Sync events to manage and monitor the flow of epics. The Value Management Office (VMO) is responsible for maintaining the effectiveness of data coming in from the value streams and the Epic Owners to feed the decision-making process within the portfolio Kanban.

The Minimum Viable Product (MVP) sub-state has an implicit WIP limit—the available capacity of the Agile Release Train doing the work, and the allocated budget for this work.

The Kanban's design should evolve to match the needs of a specific portfolio and be updated to reflect continuous improvements to the process. These improvements may include adjusting WIP limits, splitting or combining Kanban states, or adding service classes to optimize the flow and priority of epics.

What are the states of the portfolio Kanban?

Funnel

The funnel is the intake state for all the portfolio's significant business and technology ideas. These ideas originate as strategic concerns, arise from Agile Teams or ARTs, or are suggestions from customers and partners. While they can arise from any source, Figure 2 illustrates how epics typically flow into the funnel:

Figure 2. Feeding the portfolio Funnel

Maintaining and updating the portfolio vision and roadmap identifies new initiatives that feed directly into the portfolio Kanban. These may include updates from the organization's strategy or support other portfolios. The continuous exploration process within each ART also uncovers user and market needs and may result in identifying potential new epics.

At this stage, epics are initially described with a hypothesis statement. The funnel is not WIP-limited, as these epics are ideas that may deserve additional consideration. If an initial review of an idea is not likely to exceed the epic threshold guardrail or does not need to be a portfolio concern, it moves to the ART or Solution Train Kanban.

Reviewing

Since epics are among the most significant portfolio investments, an Epic Owner is needed to sponsor the epic and define its intent. Working with relevant stakeholders to elaborate on the epic hypothesis statement, they pull the epic into the reviewing state.

A WIP limit for this state is typically specified. However, if an Epic Owner is unavailable to do the work, the limit may be implicit. A WSJF estimate relative to other items in the reviewing state is established based on preliminary sizing and cost estimates. If the epic does not seem viable or aligned with the portfolio's strategic themes or other guardrails, it moves to the done state, freeing the capacity for more promising alternatives.

Analyzing

When the Epic Owner has the capacity the WIP limits allow it, epics with high potential are pulled into the analyzing state. Epics that get this far deserve more rigorous analysis and further investment. This state usually requires active collaboration among Business Owners, Enterprise and System and Solution Architects, Product and Solution Management, and Agile Teams.

During the analysis state, a Lean business case is created. The following activities inform the information captured in this business case:

Identifying and reviewing solution alternatives
Defining the MVP
Establishing refined cost estimates for both the MVP and the full implementation
Small research spikes to demonstrate potential technical and business viability
Initial customer validation

Typically, few epics are in this state, and Portfolio Leadership reviews them regularly. Since the epic's analysis and eventual implementation consume precious capacity, approval to move into the next state is more rigorous. WSJF is a factor, but there are many other things to consider. Portfolio Leadership uses the Lean Business Case during the portfolio sync to make a 'Go/No-go' decision. 'Go' confirms that the epic is approved for implementation. 'No-go' moves the epic to 'done.'

Ready

Epics in the analyzing state with the highest WSJF are pulled into the ready state as soon as space is available. This is a low-cost waiting state where epics are periodically reviewed and prioritized by updating WSJF and other relevant factors.

Implementing

Many companies come up with more good ideas than they can afford to fund. To decide which big epics to work on, Portfolio Leadership and other key stakeholders use techniques such as participatory budgeting (PB). This process helps Portfolio Leadership understand how to adjust their budgets to support the most promising initiatives.

The epics are also organized by sequencing, sizing, and ranking them against each other using WSJF. The ranking is essential to stay within the budget limits and consider how much work can be handled, investment timeframes, and other factors. Portfolio Leadership should know how much capacity the ART has, as the time needed for each epic can change based on how much work can be done.

The implementation step has two sub-states, MVP and Persevere, described in the following sections.

Implementing – MVP

To begin developing the epic's MVP, the Epic Owner works with various stakeholders to split the epic into features and capabilities. The ARTs and Solution Trains pull the work into their respective backlogs, as illustrated in Figure 3. When these features and capabilities are ready for implementation, they are presented at PI Planning events.

Figure 3. ARTs and Solution Trains pull work into their backlogs

The Epic Owner then works with the Agile Teams to begin the activities needed to develop the MVP and evaluate its business outcome hypothesis. Work continues until the money allocated for the MVP has been spent or the hypothesis can be evaluated. Portfolio Leadership evaluates the MVP's progress based on leading indicators, value stream KPIs, and demos.

If the value stream runs out of money to implement the MVP, and the need for the epic still exists, a new one can be proposed and placed in the funnel. The original epic then moves to done. Of course, LPM can adapt this rule (and any others) to meet the needs of their organization.

Implementing – Persevere

If the hypothesis is proven true, the epic advances to the persevere state. Teams will then continue implementing additional features and capabilities. ARTs manage the extra investment locally by prioritizing features in the ART backlog. Eventually, the epic will be 'done enough,' and the ongoing WSJF applied to the ART backlog will prioritize new capabilities and features from other Epics as a higher priority.

Done

Completing the fully envisioned scope from the Lean business case is not the measure of being done. Instead, the epic is done if one of the following conditions is met:

It is removed from the portfolio Kanban by Portfolio Leadership in any of the earlier states
It has achieved sufficient knowledge or has delivered appropriate value
The hypothesis is proven, and Portfolio Leadership has determined that additional portfolio governance is no longer required

If the hypothesis is proven, the various ARTs responsible for its implementation may continue working on the epic. The Epic Owner may need to provide ongoing guidance and follow-up. Since the epic is no longer a portfolio concern, leading indicators, value stream KPIs, and guardrails are used to keep the VMO and Portfolio Leadership informed of progress.
