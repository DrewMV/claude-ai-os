Customer Centricity

The most important single thing is to focus obsessively on the customer. Our goal is to be earth's most customer-centric company.

– Jeff Bezos

Definition: Customer Centricity is a mindset that focuses on creating positive experiences for the customer through the full set of products and services that the enterprise offers.

Summary

Customer centricity focuses on understanding and meeting the customer's needs. Customer-centric organizations increase profits, enhance employee engagement, and ensure customer satisfaction. These outcomes apply not only to enterprises but also to nonprofits and the public sector. Placing the customer's needs first cultivates loyalty and collaboration and benefits everyone involved.

What is customer centricity?

Customer centricity is a way of thinking that helps organizations make choices based on a deep understanding of their customers. It encourages teams to focus on customers' needs, aligning their goals with the greatest opportunities. Rather than just responding to feature requests, organizations invest time in identifying deeper, ongoing needs. The aim is to see things from the customer's perspective, thinking and feeling like they do. This enables organizations to create products and solutions that deliver immediate benefits as well as long-term customer experiences. By moving past a one-time transaction mindset, successful companies aim to build lasting relationships by understanding where their customers find value.

SAFe defines two types of customers.

Internal customers are part of the company. They use products and solutions developed by one or more development value streams and leverage them in one or more operational value streams. For example, a team of underwriting managers at a bank may be internal customers of a credit scoring solution created by the IT department. (Figure 1)
External customers operate outside of the company. They purchase, license, or use products and solutions to benefit their organization. Figure 1 shows external customers using solutions in the operational value stream to submit loan applications and repay loans. The relationship between internal and external customers may be business-to-business (B2B), business-to-consumer (B2C), or business-to-professional (B2P).

It is also often the case that multiple customers exist at various points along the development value streams that are collaborating to build products and solutions, as shown in Figure 2. Each oversees a solution that offers a valuable product or service that is either consumed or further enhanced by the immediate customer. This scenario is especially common in the development of large solutions.

What is whole product thinking?

Customer-centric organizations embrace a holistic approach to crafting products and solutions. Whole-product thinking encourages teams to see a product as more than an assembly of features. It inspires developers to consider the customer's entire experience, from purchase to use and through support. This transforms how they approach products and the value they bring to people's lives.

At a minimum, companies need to deliver the core product. However, customers will not be delighted if it's missing critical aspects of the ownership experience. Suppose you buy a smartphone that doesn't have a headphone jack (or Bluetooth), or you have to buy a separate charger, battery, or SIM card to operate the phone. Customers would be unhappy. Similarly, your customers will be dissatisfied if you have an excellent phone with no warranty or customer support or if it doesn't connect to WIFI. "You can have the most amazing product in the world, but if you don't pay attention to the Whole Product Concept, it may fail." [1]

Developing a complete product requires understanding the real benefits customers can expect to receive or experience from it. After that, consider all aspects of the customer's experience. This begins with purchase and continues through first use, ongoing user experience, maintenance, add-ons, and accessories—even how the product may be upgraded and supported.

Customers are only satisfied if the product's actual value is the same or exceeds their perceived value. Kotler, Levitt, and Moore devised variations of a whole-product model that recognizes the levels of customer needs. Figure 3 shows a version of this model, adapted for SAFe.

Figure 3. The whole product model [1]

MVP – stands for Minimum Viable Product. It is an early, basic version of a new product created to prove or disprove the benefit hypothesis. The MVP helps answer: Do customers and users want the solution? Can it be built? The SAFe MVP is an actual product that real customers can use and allows the company to generate insights to determine the core product's features and beyond. The MVP is not a whole product. It's a starting point that proves the hypothesis.

Core product – addresses the basic functional needs of the customer. It's adequate to accomplish the jobs to be done minimally but lacks certain features or attributes of a product that the customer would expect.

Expected product – provides the attributes buyers usually expect and agree to when purchasing a product. It includes the core product and other benefits the customer would expect. For example, the product has online help, documentation, customer support during the warranty period, and so on.

Augmented product – provides additional features, benefits, attributes, or related services that differentiate the product from its competitors. These are added to delight customers. For example, a laptop comes with free third-party add-ons, such as a password manager, VPN, touch screen, and more.

Potential product – focuses on the features and qualities needed to keep customers interested for the long term. It relies on market and user research, helping with strategic planning and creating lasting advantages for the product.

How do customers perceive value?

To create effective and lasting products and solutions, it's crucial to understand how customers see value. For example, consider an organization that identifies a customer problem costing around $800,000 to solve. If the customer believes the solution is worth less than that amount, the company will have difficulty selling it for a profit. Conversely, the organization could make more money if customers see more than $800,000 in value.

How does research create customer-centric products and solutions?

As shown in Figure 5, market research helps drive strategy, and user research drives design. These create the foundations of a customer-centric organization. They create actionable insights into customers' problems, the context in which the product or solution will be used, and the desired requirements.

Figure 5. Customer-centric approaches to strategy and design

Researching with empathy sets aside preconceived ideas and helps create products and solutions from the customers' perspective. It motivates teams to understand and experience the world from the customer's viewpoint. They learn and appreciate the difficulties customers face, their roles, and their context.

Customer-centric companies apply empathic research techniques throughout the entire product lifecycle, guiding the development of products and solutions that move beyond functional needs and address:

Aesthetic and emotional needs
Ergonomic requirements, such as the placement of physical features
Attributes that users may not explicitly request, such as performance, security, and compliance, but are essential for viability
Operations and ongoing maintenance

How do customer needs influence solution design?

Because no single customer represents every person or their work context, no single solution category captures every customer's needs. General solutions

To create valid solutions that serve a broad audience, it's necessary to consider the needs of a general group of customers. The Product and Solution Management teams act as their representatives to ensure customer feedback is heard and new ideas are tested. Customers don't often participate in planning meetings, so their input comes from workshops, focus groups, usability tests, and early beta releases.

Customer-built solutions

External customers work together with Product and Solution Management to design custom solutions. While the customer decides what the solution will be, the solution builder makes it happen. To guarantee everything works smoothly, they must cooperate, coordinate, and negotiate deliverables, timing, and other details.

Deep and narrow solutions

Deep and narrow solutions are the middle ground between general and custom solutions. A small number of customers will often pay a premium price for these products and services. For example, a solution to manage logistics for NFL stadiums with more than 50,000 people has a potential market of fewer than 32 customers.

Multi-segment solutions

Some solutions serve different markets, each using the solution slightly differently. In this situation, customer-centricity requires understanding the unique needs of each segment:

B2B – Members of a B2B partner segment may act more like customers of custom-built solutions, each making specific requests of the software provider to adjust, extend, or improve the API to meet their unique needs better.

B2C – A B2C software company serving hundreds of thousands to millions of indirect customers via a website may also offer a set of developer APIs to partners.

How do customer-centric organizations leverage market rhythms and events?

The Lean-Agile mindset drives the continuous and sustainable flow of value to customers. This motivates organizations to understand how the timing of releases influences their perceived value. In other words, the value of a product can vary significantly based on its release date. To create the highest value for their stakeholders, customer-centric organizations leverage market rhythms and market events [1]:

Market rhythms

A market rhythm is a set of events repeatedly occurring on a predictable cadence. For example, retailers routinely prepare for the holiday shopping season by upgrading their systems to gain a competitive edge and support significantly higher transaction volumes. Market rhythms help organizations recognize and capitalize on predictable opportunities that require longer-term planning. Market events

A market event is a one-time event that is likely to affect one or more solutions materially. It can be external, such as the launch of government regulations, or internally created, such as a company's annual user conference. Key Takeaways:
Customer centricity is a mindset that focuses on understanding customer needs.
Whole product thinking guides organizations to consider the needs of customers throughout the entire product lifecycle.
Customer centricity guides all aspects of research, design, and delivery.
Different types of customers have different perceptions of value and different needs.
