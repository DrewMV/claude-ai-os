Scrum Master/Team Coach

A Scrum Master is like an orchestra conductor, guiding a group of individuals to create something that none of them alone could create.

—Mike Cohn, Paraphrased from Succeeding with Agile [1]

Definition: The SAFe Scrum Master/Team Coach (SM/TC) is a servant leader and coach for an Agile Team who facilitates team events and processes, and supports teams and ARTs in delivering value.

Summary

The SAFe Scrum Master /Team Coach (SM/TC) is a servant leader who supports and facilitates the team events and processes that deliver value. They coach Agile team members in Scrum, Kanban, and quality practices. The SM/TC guides teams through planning, execution, review, and reflection on their work. They also collaborate across Agile Teams to enhance workflow efficiency. As servant leaders, SM/TCs prioritize the needs of their team members and ensure alignment with organizational values and goals. They mentor teammates to cultivate a continuous learning mindset and guide people to seek solutions independently.

What is a Scrum Master/Team Coach?

The Scrum Master/Team Coach (SM/TC) is a key member of the Agile Team who assists the team in meeting their delivery goals. They coach self-organization and self-management of practices and processes. They help Agile Teams coordinate and participate in Agile Release Train (ART) events.

SM/TCs are responsible for helping the Agile Team increase its effectiveness within the organization. Coaching SAFe Scrum and SAFe Team Kanban supports SAFe principles and practices and helps identify and eliminate flow bottlenecks.

SM/TCs come from various backgrounds and are in high demand. Depending on the size of the team, context, and other responsibilities, the SM/TC can be a part-time or full-time role. Sometimes, a team member assumes the role along with other duties; or an experienced SM/TC can support more than one team. However, during initial SAFe adoption, the job can be more demanding.

Although typically not people managers, they are influential Agile Team members and need the following attributes.

Empathy – Empathy is vital for trust and effective coaching. SM/TCs show genuine concern for team members' beliefs and feelings. They foster relationships that enhance collaboration and performance.
Conflict navigation – SM/TCs help team members resolve interpersonal conflicts, problem-solving, and decision-making. They promote what Agile coach and author Lyssa Atkins says, "Navigating conflict is our new mindset, in which we help teams move from conflict to constructive disagreement as a catapult to high performance." [2]
Servant leadership – A servant leader influences rather than commands, focusing on the team's needs to align outcomes with organizational values. SM/TCs can choose how to collaborate, ensuring events are positive, productive, and time-bound by facilitating or allowing team members to lead. Rotating responsibilities for meetings is essential to team growth and self-management.
Mentoring – To help the team develop a continuous learning mindset, SM/TCs support personal development. Instead of giving team members answers, they guide them to find solutions independently.
Transparency – Transparency is central to SAFe and vital for learning. The Scrum Master or Team Coach encourages feedback and openness, promoting transparency by reviewing work items, identifying gaps between expectations and outcomes, and pointing out recurring mistakes.
Coach – The SM/TC understands and educates the team on methods beyond Scrum, such as SAFe, Kanban, flow, and built-in quality. They often have advanced training and experience in numerous technical and business domains.

Facilitating PI Planning

SM/TCs play a vital role in PI Planning. They collaborate with other SM/TCs and the Release Train Engineer (RTE), working with the team during PI planning. An effective SM/TC is critical to a successful event, typically performing the following activities to facilitate PI planning:

Prepare for PI Planning – Before the event, the SM/TC ensures that Product Managers, Business Owners, and other stakeholders brief the team on upcoming features, as illustrated in Figure 2. They help Agile Teams and the Product Owner (PO) identify the right amount of local stories, maintenance, defects, tech debt, and other work to enable meaningful discussion across the ART during PI Planning.
Draft PI plans – The SM/TC helps develop draft PI plans for the PI's iterations, listing PI objectives and identifying ART risks and issues. They also help set up digital or physical planning areas, providing visual radiators to create transparency and collaboration. They allow the team to determine but not exceed their capacity.
Coordinate with other teams – SM/TCs ensure cooperation and communication during PI planning. They may secure subject matter experts (SMEs) and ART stakeholders. They foster discussions with other teams to determine how best to collaborate on feature development and resolve dependencies.
Create team PI objectives – SM/TCs help teams create their objectives for the upcoming PI. They ensure the team documents objectives before the draft plan review, including a mix of committed and uncommitted goals.
Review final plans and business value – Before the final review, the SM/TCs ensure that PI objectives are SMART—Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, and time-bound. They aid collaboration between the Business Owner and the team to define business value.

Supporting Iteration execution

SM/TCs support Agile Teams during the iteration, increasing the likelihood of achieving their iteration goals and PI objectives. For example, they:

Facilitate team events – Agile Teams use cadence-based events to coordinate and sync their efforts. While Scrum and Team Kanban operate somewhat differently, all teams need to plan, sync, review, inspect their work, and hold retrospectives.
Work within the ART's cadence – SM/TCs help teams apply Scrum or Kanban within the ART's development cadence and synchronization requirements. This facilitates alignment, dependency management, release-on-demand, and fast integrated learning cycles (SAFe Principle #4).
Collaborate with the PO – The PO is accountable for maximizing the value of the team's work. So, supporting the PO is part of the SM/TC's role. They do this by:
Helping the team understand and apply tools and techniques for customer centricity and design thinking to build the right thing at the right time.
Ensuring the team understands the need for clear and concise team backlog items and aligns to the ART's capacity allocation for each work item type.
Helping the team apply empirical planning and development to evaluate progress by observing and testing working solutions in small increments.
Facilitating stakeholder collaboration as requested or needed.

Improving flow

The SM/TC can significantly improve team workflow by eliminating bottlenecks, delays, and waste. This coaching often includes the following activities:

Establish the team Kanban board – SAFe teams use a Kanban board to visualize their work and enhance flow. An effective Kanban system is one adapted to a specific Agile Team and based on the work it performs (marketing, software development, and hardware development). Other influences are the team members' skills and their role in the ART. The best way to create the Kanban system is to involve the entire Agile Team, guided by an experienced coach SM/TC.
Measure and optimize flow – SM/TCs help the team establish metrics to assess and improve performance. Specific measures for flow, competency, and outcomes are described in the article 'Measure and Grow.' Flow metrics help the SM/TC and the team evolve their process iteratively and continuously adapt to the team's needs. After defining the initial process and WIP limits and executing them for a while, bottlenecks should become visible. If not, the team refines the process or reduces some WIP limits until it becomes evident that a workflow state is overloaded or starving.
Build quality in – Agile Teams operate in a fast, flow-based system to quickly develop and release high-quality business capabilities. The SM/TC assists by coaching Built-in Quality practices. This enables fast, reliable execution and ensures that teams perform necessary changes regularly and efficiently.

Building high-performing Teams

Creating a healthy Agile Team is essential to generate high-value increments of working solutions. Fortunately, many of the necessary ingredients are built into SAFe by design. For example, SAFe Agile Teams are small, cross-functional, and self-organizing. They're empowered to define and execute the work needed to accomplish the team's and the ART's objectives. Team members agree that increments should meet a shared, scalable definition of done (DoD). SM/TCs play a critical role in building high-performing teams to accomplish this through the following activities:

Foster Agile Team attributes – High-performing teams self-manage and work toward clear, shared goals. This requires alignment, collaboration, healthy conflict resolution, and diversity. SM/TCs support these attributes by providing timely feedback and promoting open communication among all members of the ART, including key decision-makers. Engaged and motivated SAFe teams enjoy their work and collaborate effectively.
Encourage high-performing team dynamics – SM/TCs foster a high-performance environment of continuous flow and relentless improvement. They mentor the team to create an atmosphere of mutual respect. This helps resolve interpersonal conflicts and identifies growth opportunities. SM/TCs focus the team on creating increments of high value for each iteration.
Become a more effective Scrum Master/Team Coach – Servant leaders understand that growth comes from nurturing the people who deliver the results. This is how SM/TCs serve the team and the larger organization. They facilitate the adoption of SAFe across the organization by coaching stakeholders and non-agile teams on interacting effectively with Agile Teams. Additionally, they participate in the SM/TC Community of Practice (COP) and support the organization's SAFe Practice Consultants (SPCs).
Serve as Lean-Agile leaders – SM/TCs lead by example and incorporate the Lean-Agile Mindset and SAFe Lean-Agile Principles into their responsibilities. They model behavior for others to follow.
Foster collaboration on the team – SM/TCs offer observations, feedback, guidance, and advice based on what they know and have seen succeed. This fosters more effective and cohesive teams, producing better outcomes, solutions, and products.
Coach with powerful questions – SM/TCs recognize they do not have all the answers. Instead, they ask powerful questions to uncover what's essential, guiding others to use their knowledge and expertise. Some examples include:
How did we get to this point?
What other options should we consider?
What are we not seeing?
What should we do to gain more insight?
How would you proceed if success was certain?

SM/TCs ask these questions to help teams improve performance. Teams learn to solve problems independently, make better decisions, learn new skills, and reach their goals.

Resolve team conflicts – Teamwork is the ultimate competitive advantage. However, many teams are dysfunctional, according to Patrick Lencioni, author of 'Five Dysfunctions of a Team.' In his book, Lencioni suggests that an absence of trust leads to the other four dysfunctions. [4] SM/TCs address them with the SAFe practices illustrated in Figure 5.
Develop team skillsets – SM/TCs work with team members and their functional managers to help them develop 'T-shaped' skills. A 'T-shaped' individual has general expertise in many areas and is an expert in one of them. SM/TCs encourage team members to pair with others to expand their skills, take on tasks in another discipline and business domain, take courses, and read books to become continuous learners.

Improving ART performance

SM/TCs help Agile Teams improve the overall ART performance with the following activities:

Facilitate cross-team collaboration – To bring whole-product solutions to market, Agile Teams cooperate across the organization. SAFe SM/TCs create an environment where collaboration thrives and practices support opportunities for teams to work together, for example:
Aligning to ART PI objectives, vision, and strategic themes during PI Planning and addressing dependencies using the ART board
Representing the team in the Coach Sync, PO Sync, and ART Syncs
Attending another team's events and demos with relevant team members
Participating in the ART's System Demos and I&A events

One significant benefit of working across teams is that colleagues learn from one another. Learning new skills makes everyone more valuable to the organization and better equipped to support each other's work. It also guards against specialty skills becoming a bottleneck, which increases delays and reduces quality.

Build trust with stakeholders – The SM/TC helps the team build trust. SAFe relies on a rolling wave of short-term commitments from Agile Teams and ARTs to assist with business planning and outcomes. This increases alignment and trust between development and organization stakeholders. While solution development is uncertain by nature, the organization depends on teams for some measure of reliable, predictable forecasting. Too little predictability inhibits planning. Too much leads to longer-term plans, which are unreliable and limit agility. The organization and technology stakeholders need something in between, which is the purpose of PI objectives.
Coach the IP Iteration – SM/TCs help ensure the team does not schedule any work for the IP Iteration during PI planning. Instead, they coach teams to use this iteration as an estimating buffer for meeting PI objectives and dedicating time for innovation, continuing education, PI planning, and the Inspect and Adapt (I&A) events.
Help the team Inspect & Adapt – The SM/TC helps prepare the team for the I&A event. This includes the PI System Demo, quantitative and qualitative measurement, and the retrospective and problem-solving workshop. They guide the team in I&A activities and stay within the allotted timeboxes.
Facilitate the problem-solving workshop – SM/TCs coach teams in root cause analysis, the 'five whys' [5], and Pareto analysis. They ensure the work needed to deliver the identified improvements is planned and added to the Agile Team's backlog.

Key Takeaways
The Scrum Master/Team Coach (SM/TC) helps Agile Teams meet their delivery goals.
The SM/TC improves Agile Release Train (ART) performance, facilitates PI Planning, supports iterations, and builds high-performing teams.
By eliminating bottlenecks, delays, and waste, the SM/TC can improve team workflow.
The SM/TC plays a critical role in PI Planning by collaborating with other SM/TCs and the Release Train Engineer (RTE).
