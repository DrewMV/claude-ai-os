Iterations

None of my inventions came by accident. I see a worthwhile need to be met, and I make trial after trial until it comes.

– Thomas Edison

Definition: Iterations are a standard, fixed-duration timebox during which Agile Teams and ARTs individually and collectively deliver incremental customer value while working towards the PI objectives.

Summary

Agile Teams and ARTs fulfill their responsibilities by working in a series of iterations. Each iteration is a Plan-Do-Check-Adjust (PDCA) cycle. Iterations are continuous and sequential, and a new iteration starts immediately after the previous one. Iterations use fixed-duration timeboxes during which Agile Teams and ARTs deliver incremental customer value while working towards PI objectives. A typical PI has four two-week development iterations, followed by one Innovation and Planning iteration. Agile Teams collaborate within a PI to advance the product or solution toward the team and ART PI objectives. All teams on the ART synchronize to the same iteration and PI cadence, enabling them to explore, integrate, deploy, and release value together and independently.

What is an iteration?

An iteration is a set amount of time in which Agile Teams work together to provide value to their customers. A PI commonly comprises four two-week development iterations and one Innovation and Planning (IP) iteration. During the IP iteration, teams can explore new ideas and evaluate product design options. This time helps teams meet their goals and allows creativity, learning, planning, and reviews. All teams on the ART work to the same iteration cadence, making it easier to collaborate and synchronize their work.

How is the PDCA cycle applied within iterations?

Each iteration generally breaks down into four steps: Plan, Do, Check, and Adjust (PDCA). First, the team identifies what tasks or goals they want to accomplish in that iteration during the "Plan" phase. Next comes the "Do" phase, where the team carries out their planned tasks. They focus on completing the work efficiently and collaborating effectively. Then, the team moves to the "Check" phase, reviewing what they accomplished and looking closely at what went well and what didn't. Finally, in the "Adjust" step, the team discusses the feedback and decides how to improve their process or products for the next iteration. This cycle continues with a new iteration starting immediately after the previous one ends.

Whether teams use SAFe Scrum, SAFe Team Kanban, or a mix of both, the goal is the same: keep a consistent rhythm to help with planning and teamwork. This connects to the Plan-Do-Check-Act cycle. Scrum teams have specific events to plan (Iteration Planning), carry out tasks (Do), review their progress (Iteration Review), and make changes if needed (Iteration Retrospective). On the other hand, Kanban teams primarily work in a continuous flow but can also use specific events as desired. Either method reflects the idea of continually improving, which is at the heart of the PDCA cycle.

PI PDCA Cycle

PI Planning starts the PDCA cycle, where teams partially plan the iterations in the PI to achieve the product or solution vision and deliver the highest priority features. The ART measures progress with a regular series of System Demos. The timebox ends with the PI System Demo, the first part of the Inspect and Adapt (I&A) event. This demo shows all the features the ART has developed during the entire PI and acts as the check step. During the I&A, Business Owners score PI objectives and assess product and solution performance. Additionally, everyone in the ART collaborates to identify and address systemic problems in the problem-solving workshop part of the I&A, fulfilling the adjust step of the PDCA cycle.

How do ART events apply the PDCA cycle within iterations?

Getting feedback is critical to an ART's high development velocity. Fast learning, frequent integration points, and adaptation within the ART iteration PDCA cycles generate speed.

Each iteration is a short PDCA cycle in which the ART develops a significant portion of the PI objectives. Iterations apply SAFe Principle #4, Build incrementally with fast, integrated learning cycles.

The following sections describe the PDCA activities in the ART's iterations.

Plan

Planning for the system demo – Teams and ART stakeholders work together to decide what to show and who will present at the system demo. But first, they need to agree on the system's setup, logistics, and staging environment. Product Management then shares the agenda and details of the demo with stakeholders, including those interested in the new features from a business or customer perspective.
Planning for releases – Teams will regularly add new features to the product or solution and release updates in the next iteration. The Agile Release Train (ART) needs to ensure that everything meets its quality and compliance standards and consider how this will affect the business and customers.

Do

Delivering value – When teams finish building new features, they share them with customers to complete the process. The Agile Release Train (ART) can automate many of the releases through the Continuous Delivery Pipeline, but sometimes they need to execute steps manually. Then, the ART might also need to talk to stakeholders and involve them in the delivery process.
Removing impediments and addressing risks – The Release Train Engineer (RTE), Product Management, and System Architect work with teams during the iteration to address obstacles. Occasionally, they discuss concerns with Business Owners and other stakeholders.
Executing the system demo – The system demo allows everyone to check the ART's work at the end of each iteration. It helps team members evaluate accomplishments, gather feedback, and improve the product and the development process.

Check

Conducting sync events – The ART holds synchronization events, including PO and Coach Syncs. These events ensure progress, help resolve cross-team and ART dependencies, and solve other problems.
Measuring and monitoring progress – Progress towards PI objectives is measured and monitored. The ROAM board created during PI planning is typically reviewed during the PO or Coach Syncs. This confirms that parties responsible for owning or mitigating a risk take the necessary actions.
Reviewing the progress of improvements from the previous I&A: The RTE and Scrum Master/Team Coaches (SM/TCs) collaborate to ensure the ART accomplishes the I&A improvements identified during the PI.
Reviewing outcome and flow metrics – SAFe has three measurement domains: outcomes, flow, and competency. Teams review them periodically to assess progress, support better decision-making, and help identify improvement opportunities for the ART's people, products, and processes.

Adjust

Addressing feedback – Rapid feedback from customers, Business Owners, and other stakeholders is essential. Their insights guide the ART in building feasible, desirable, and sustainable products and solutions.

Continually refine the ART Backlog – The ART backlog is regularly prioritized and updated to ensure teams progress toward their PI goals. This confirms that customers will benefit from the solutions they are working on.

Improving the ART's processes – Measuring progress and gaining fast feedback provides the data the ART needs to improve the development process.

How do Agile Team events apply the PDCA cycle within iterations?

Agile Teams deliver a fast, reliable flow of customer value by executing a full plan-do-check-adjust (PDCA) cycle each iteration. Their activities include:

Gathering and actioning on feedback is essential for each Agile Team to deliver value. Each iteration serves as a short PDCA cycle in which each Agile Team develops and integrates progress against its objectives.

The following sections describe the PDCA activities in the ART's iterations.

Plan

Refining the Team Backlog – Talking with customers and other stakeholders helps teams improve their backlogs. It reduces confusion between the business and developers, identifies dependencies, and reduces wasted time and delays. Having clear criteria for accepting stories makes it easier to understand requirements, allowing everyone to share their ideas and knowledge. This encourages team engagement and responsibility.
Team iteration planning – SAFe Scrum teams work together to plan at the start of each cycle during the Iteration Planning meeting. They decide how much work they can handle from their backlog in that period and record it. Then, they create iteration goals to summarize their plan. SAFe Kanban teams, on the other hand, plan as needed, usually every week. They coordinate their work, add new stories to their backlog, and handle any dependencies or deadlines for the iteration. Establishing iteration goals helps everyone share objectives, track progress, manage dependencies, and make adjustments during the PI.
Planning for team and system demos – Teams plan how to demo the stories they defined during and after PI planning. This helps them implement features and meet their PI objectives. 'Beginning with the end in mind' allows planning and alignment, fostering a thorough understanding of the functionality needed before the iteration execution.

Do

Delivering stories and high-value quality increments of value – Teams use a Continuous Delivery Pipeline (CDP) to ensure that they can consistently explore, integrate, and deploy their work.
Conducting team sync and demos – Teams continuously coordinate and synchronize their work, inspecting progress and creating an action plan to coordinate current and upcoming work. They demo completed work immediately to get fast feedback.
Monitoring the progress of stories – Teams use a Kanban board to visualize all active and pending work, workflow states, WIP limits, risks, and impediments.

Check

Conducting retrospectives – Teams reflect on the iteration and develop new ideas to improve the increment and process. Depending on the method, they may do this periodically at the Iteration Retrospective. Retrospectives ensure continuous improvements, updating the problem-solving part of the I&A with the knowledge gained.
Reviewing improvement stories from prior iterations – Teams review the results of their previous improvement stories. This affirms they've sufficiently addressed the identified problems and their root causes.
Reviewing demos with the team's stakeholders – Teams demo their completed work as soon as possible to get fast Customer and stakeholder feedback. Scrum teams typically conduct a more formal Iteration Review to better understand their progress.

Adjust

Improving the team's processes – Based on the outcome, flow, and competency metrics, the team identifies its process improvements and addresses impediments and delays that otherwise interrupt the flow of value.

Refining the team backlog – Teams continually refine their backlog to ensure it always contains some stories ready for implementation. During refinement, teams review upcoming user stories and features to discuss, estimate, and establish preliminary acceptance criteria.

Key Takeaways
Iterations are set periods of time for delivering incremental value.
Each iteration is a full Plan-Do-Check-Adjust (PDCA) cycle.
Agile Teams deliver a fast, reliable flow of customer value each iteration.
Teams and ART stakeholders collaborate to decide what to show at the system demo.
