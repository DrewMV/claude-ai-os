Lean Governance

Summary

Lean governance is a critical aspect of Lean Portfolio Management (LPM), which provides oversight for spending, audits, compliance, and other key activities. Effective Lean governance requires Portfolio Leadership to collaborate with key roles, including the Value Management Office (VMO), Lean-Agile Center of Excellence (LACE), Business Owners, and Enterprise Architects.

Key activities included in Lean governance include forecasting and budgeting dynamically, measuring portfolio performance, and coordinating continuous compliance. Compliance, particularly in the context of Agile development, requires a shift towards automated governance to manage risk and ensure adherence to regulations.

What is Lean governance in a SAFe Portfolio?

Modernizing portfolio management is critical to supporting the Lean-Agile way of working and competing in this new reality. Lean governance continues to be a critical need, supporting oversight of spending, audit, compliance, expenditure, measurement, and reporting.

Portfolio Leadership is generally a team of individuals across business, technology, and finance. This team is often the executive team in smaller organizations with a single portfolio. In larger organizations with multiple portfolios, the team is often comprised of the senior leaders accountable for each portfolio. They play a crucial role in overseeing Lean Portfolio Management and ensuring that the portfolio's work aligns with its strategic themes and financial goals and those of the broader organization.

Lean governance provides oversight of spending, audit, security, compliance, expenditure, measurement, and reporting. Effective Lean governance requires the Portfolio Leadership Team to collaborate with the VMO, LACE, Business Owners, and Enterprise Architects.

How does Lean governance help Portfolio Leaders forecast and budget dynamically?

Every SAFe portfolio operates within an approved budget for developing and deploying systems and Solutions that the Enterprise needs to meet its strategic objectives. The portfolio's total budget is allocated to individual value streams by Portfolio Leadership and portfolio stakeholders. The value stream's budget funds the people and resources to help achieve the current Portfolio vision and roadmap.

SAFe provides a dynamic approach to budgeting. This lightweight, more fluid, Agile process replaces traditional planning, fixed long-range budget cycles, financial commitments, and scope. This new model includes understanding each solution's historical and forecasted future costs and epics. LPM adjusts budgets on a cadence, typically at PI boundaries or when significant events warrant, as part of the strategic portfolio review.

Portfolio Leadership establishes guardrails that help ensure that the mix of investments across a Portfolio addresses both near-term opportunities and long-term strategy. Alongside the VMO, they measure and track the investment across the Portfolio's value streams to ensure that investments in technology, infrastructure, and maintenance are being developed and that significant new investments are approved appropriately. This style of governance empowers decentralized decision-making across ARTs and Agile Teams on how best to implement the opportunities while ensuring the blend of work needed is occurring.

SAFe provides Lean budgeting strategies that eliminate traditional project-based funding and cost accounting overhead. In this model, LPM maintains appropriate levels of oversight through allocating value stream budgets and applying Lean budget guardrails. This empowers decentralized decision-making while maintaining financial governance.

This approach offers several key advantages to the Portfolio:

Dynamic budgeting: This agility ensures that resources are continuously aligned with strategic goals. Traditional budgeting often follows a rigid annual cycle, but Lean governance enables flexible and adaptable budgeting. This allows leaders to rapidly reallocate funds to support the most valuable initiatives as market conditions shift, new opportunities emerge, or organizational priorities evolve.
Continuous measurement: Regular assessment of portfolio performance is central to Lean governance, and by tracking key metrics and indicators, leaders gain insights into the health and progress of each Value Stream. This data-driven approach allows for the early identification of underperforming areas or potential bottlenecks, enabling leaders to take proactive steps to address issues and optimize outcomes.
Collaboration: Effective LPM requires close collaboration and alignment between portfolio leaders and stakeholders. This includes regular communication to ensure a shared understanding of strategic objectives, priorities, and resource allocation. By fostering a collaborative environment, leaders can build trust and gain buy-in for funding decisions.
Real-Time visibility: Lean governance emphasizes the importance of transparency and visibility into portfolio performance. By actively participating in activities like backlog refinement and PI System Demos, Portfolio Leadership and Epic Owners gain first-hand insights into the work being done and the value being delivered. This real-time visibility enables data-informed decision-making and rapid course correction when needed.
Adjusting Value Stream budgets: Lean governance recognizes that priorities and market conditions can change rapidly. As a result, budgets should not be static. Portfolio Leadership, with data gathered by the VMO, should regularly reassess Value Stream performance and adjust funding levels accordingly. This ensures that resources are allocated to the areas of highest value and impact.

How does Lean governance ensure continuous compliance?

Many enterprises are significantly challenged to manage their risk and compliance exposure in the face of ever-increasing challenges. The volume of regulations can be massive, the risks are significant, the data sources and tools are numerous, and the relationships between these entities are complex. It's too much for traditional processes and infrastructure to handle effectively. Moreover, conventional compliance processes tend to defer these activities to the end, subjecting the enterprise to late discovery of issues, subsequent rework, and even compromising compliance. Therefore, a more continuous approach is recommended to coordinate ongoing compliance with relevant standards.

Moreover, as organizations adopt DevOps, the delivery rate for digital products races forward, making it extremely challenging for compliance to keep pace without getting in the way. Compliance workarounds and exceptions abound, which may result in regulatory or legal exposure and, worse, organizations being shut down or taken over by the government for non-compliance.

"Organizations need an automated way to track governance throughout the entire software delivery process so they can attest to the integrity of all assets and the security of all running applications."

—Investments Unlimited [1]

So, how can you ensure that all aspects of your deployment pipeline are protected as delivery velocity dramatically increases? Companies that automate governance, risk, and compliance can achieve organizational goals better and faster while improving flow, reducing rework, and meeting regulations.

The DevOps Automated Governance Reference Architecture paper offers organizations guidance to help them design and implement automated governance throughout the Continuous Delivery Pipeline. This seminal whitepaper illustrates new strategies for automating significant elements of compliance and governance. The white paper inspired the book Investments Unlimited, which advances these innovative ideas and tells a story in novel form. This work and others give organizations a starting plan to automate compliance and shift compliance risk left.

Big Data Security

Every enterprise uses data to improve its products, optimize operations, and better understand its customers and markets. Big data concerns are addressed at the portfolio level as it requires vision, investment, and governance within and across the value streams within the portfolio. Big data governance manages the availability, usability, integrity, and security of the data in enterprise systems based on internal data standards and policies controlling data usage. Effective data governance ensures that data is consistent and trustworthy and doesn't get misused.

Responsible AI

Within a scaled organization, implementing responsible AI practices usually begins as a top-priority initiative for senior executives and the board of directors. The initial leader of a company's responsible AI initiative is often a C-level leader, with oversight from a special advisory group created by the board of directors to guide the AI strategy and ensure AI is used responsibly.

When the responsible AI effort starts, a team with members from different departments handles the detailed work of creating rules and practices for responsible AI use. This team often includes Portfolio Leadership to integrate responsible AI practices into each Portfolio's overall strategy and investment decisions.

Portfolio Leadership and the members of VMO will ensure alignment to any applicable set of rules and guidelines to ensure that AI is used responsibly across the Portfolio's value streams. Some rules, like policies on using AI tools on company devices and networks, are for everyone. Other rules are specific to certain roles or departments. Many guidelines help shape the company's strategy and highlight the initiatives needed to develop systems that support Responsible AI, with Portfolio Leadership playing a key role in prioritizing and funding these initiatives.
