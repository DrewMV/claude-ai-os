Customizing SAFe

An effective framework provides a coherent system of elements that work together to achieve a specific set of outcomes. Implementing these elements in a particular context is the secret to unlocking value.

– Andrew Sales, SAFe Chief Methodologist

Definition: Customizing SAFe describes the situation where new elements are added to the Framework, or the default definition and implementation of existing elements are modified.

Summary

Customizing SAFe is an activity distinct from configuring specific elements of the Framework. It recognizes that sometimes organizations need to go beyond existing definitions and processes when applying SAFe to their context. Different reasons exist for customizing SAFe, such as industry needs, organizational size, technology, culture, business goals, and continuous learning.

Customization typically falls into three categories: adaptation, improvement, and extension. Done right, customizing SAFe can unlock huge value for an organization, but it also brings some potential pitfalls. To avoid these, four guardrails are defined alongside a clear four-step process. A well-thought-out communication and implementation plan is also critical to managing the change effectively.

What is meant by customizing SAFe?

SAFe has evolved through the development and incorporation of proven practices, with each element of the Framework contributing to an overall system aimed at achieving Business Agility. At the heart of SAFe is the SAFe Big Picture, shown in Figure 1, which illustrates the operating model for applying Lean and Agile principles and practices at scale. Although SAFe is extensive and provides guidance for most circumstances, it is a Framework, and as such, it can be customized as needed.

Customizing vs Configuring SAFe

The word 'customizing' has been chosen carefully to differentiate it from 'configuring'. Many elements of SAFe are designed to be configured. Some examples include:

How Agile Release Trains (ARTs) are organized and how they are staffed
What roles are needed to create cross-functional Agile teams
Which topologies should we use to organize our teams and ARTs
Whether we use OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) to improve alignment across our portfolio
What development cadence do we want to apply
Which roles in our organization would be best suited to act as Business Owners

All of these decisions are examples of configuring SAFe; however, none of these are examples of customizing SAFe.

Customizing SAFe describes the situation where changes are made to elements of the Framework that alter their definition or application.

Common Drivers for Customizing SAFe

There are many reasons to customize SAFe. The following list includes some of the most common reasons, but it is by no means exhaustive.

Industry-specific needs: Different industries have unique regulatory requirements, operational models, and cultural nuances.
Organizational size and structure: A large, complex enterprise will have different scaling needs and challenges compared to a smaller, more Agile startup. Customization enables tailoring SAFe to the specific size, hierarchy, and existing structures of an organization.
Technological landscape and innovation: The rapid evolution of technology, especially with the rise of AI, can significantly impact development processes. Organizations may customize SAFe to integrate new tools, methodologies, or technology-driven practices that enhance their specific technological capabilities.
Existing organizational culture and maturity: Some organizations have established ways of working and a unique culture with common language and definitions already spread throughout. Customizing SAFe can help bridge the gap between the Framework and the existing organizational context, fostering smoother adoption and maintaining the positives of the existing culture.
Specific business goals and challenges: Organizations may have particular business objectives, such as accelerating time to market for a specific product line, improving cross-functional collaboration, or addressing specific bottlenecks. Customization allows them to fine-tune SAFe to directly support these unique goals.
Relentless improvement and continuous learning: As organizations operate with SAFe, they gain insights into what works best for them. This continuous learning can lead to refinements and adjustments to the Framework.

Examples of Customizing SAFe

Over the years, organizations have customized SAFe in many different ways. A good example of customizing SAFe is in the Government domain, where some of the language and terminology of the Framework, as defined, do not apply. Terms such as Business Value become Mission Value, or Customer becomes Public Servant. In fact, a SAFe for Government Big Picture and the SAFe for Government Explained eBook have evolved to support this customization.

Figure 2. Customized SAFe Big Picture for Government

Another example of customizing SAFe includes organizations that have changed the standard definition of the PI length of 8-12 weeks to run shorter 4-6 week PIs, in order to accelerate feedback and create maximum responsiveness. Another example of customizing SAFe is applying Lean Portfolio Management (LPM) practices to manage operational rather than just development initiatives or incorporating more robust risk management practices into SAFe.

How do we customize SAFe safely?

Organizations have been customizing SAFe ever since the Framework was first released. Many of these customizations worked well, but some had an adverse effect and compromised the essence of Lean and Agile at scale. For this reason, some caution is needed.

Work with Experienced Change Agents

Customizing SAFe requires a deep knowledge of the Framework and an understanding of the consequences of making a change. As such, it is beneficial to have an experienced change agent who has gone through multiple SAFe implementations to guide an organization through this process. We consider customization to be an advanced practice, and as such, it is covered in the Advanced SPC course taught by SPCTs.

Experienced change agents can help identify the right time to customize SAFe. As shown in Figure 3 below, waiting until the organization has some implementation experience can often be the right approach.

Apply the 'Customizing SAFe Guardrails'

The following guardrails should be applied to ensure that SAFe is customized safely and for maximum benefit:

GUARDRAIL 1: Enhance agility, RATHER THAN compromise the essence of SAFe

It could easily be argued that the first guardrail is the most important. It ensures that any customization does not affect the very essence of the Framework.

Three key foundational elements describe the underlying essence of SAFe and act as a mirror to hold up to each potential customization. In short, if a change invalidates the Lean-Agile Mindset, the SAFe Core Values, and/or the SAFe Principles, it should be treated with extreme caution.

The Lean Agile Mindset: The Lean-Agile Mindset combines beliefs, attitudes, and actions based on Lean Thinking and the Agile Manifesto. This mindset, emphasizing continuous improvement and flexibility, is essential for driving successful organizational transformations.

The SAFe Core Values: SAFe is built on four core values: alignment, transparency, respect for people, and relentless improvement. These values guide everyone's behavior and are foundational to Lean and Agile at scale.

The SAFe Principles: SAFe is based on ten immutable, underlying Lean-Agile principles. These principles are informed by a rich body of knowledge, including Agile, systems thinking, product development, and Lean. Any customizations should be grounded in this set of fundamental and stable principles.

Therefore, a question to ask when faced with a potential customization is:
Does this customization contradict the Lean-Agile Mindset, SAFe Core Values, and SAFe Principles?

GUARDRAIL 2: When it is the right solution, RATHER THAN the easiest thing to do

Every implementation of SAFe presents challenges that need to be overcome and opportunities to be harnessed. In some cases, customizing SAFe is the very best way to address these. However, customizing SAFe is a significant decision that should not be undertaken lightly; it should be used only when it is the best option available rather than the easiest.

A question to ask when faced with a potential customization:
Why is customization the best approach for addressing this particular challenge or opportunity?

GUARDRAIL 3: Amplify outcomes, RATHER THAN maintain the status quo

The third guardrail helps avoid situations in which customizations are made to avoid real change or maintain existing ways of working.

A question that helps to test whether this guardrail is being met is:
Which outcomes does this customization help to amplify or accelerate?

GUARDRAIL 4: Optimize systemically, RATHER THAN solve local problems

The fourth guardrail reminds us that SAFe is an integrated system. Any change needs to be validated to ensure that it works holistically.

A question that helps to ensure this guardrail has been met is:
Does this customization break or invalidate any other elements in the Framework or introduce inconsistencies that negatively impact the wider SAFe implementation?

What are the customizable elements of SAFe?

With this background, the next obvious question is: What elements of SAFe are customizable? To answer this question, it must be recognized that any system of work can be distilled down to three key categories.

The People, which describes the roles and responsibilities of roles and groups.
The Work, describes both the work items that people engage with and the workflows that govern them.
The Activities, which describe the events and interactions that people take part in.

These categories can be further decomposed into seven aspects, shown in Figure 4, which form the basis for customizing SAFe.

A step-by-step process for customizing SAFe

With this understanding, we follow a lightweight but structured process for customizing SAFe.

1. Identify the need for potentially customizing SAFe
2. Define the proposed customizations
3. Validate each customization against the guardrails
4. Identify the expected benefits and success measures

Step 1. Identify the need for potentially customizing SAFe

Although the need to customize SAFe may arise from multiple sources, such as Iteration Reviews, Inspect and Adapt events, and SAFe assessment results, a dedicated, more wide-ranging event that specifically provides time and space to reflect on the current state of the SAFe implementation is recommended. Often, this is facilitated by members of the LACE or Advanced SPCs.

Step 2. Define the proposed customization

A customization is proposed for each need. To help think through possible changes, it is useful to refer to the aspects table which describes all the aspects of the Framework that can be customized. The specific aspect being changed and the change's description are captured separately.

Step 3. Validate each customization against the guardrails

The next step is to compare each proposed customization against the four guardrails described earlier. The guardrails guide the organization in the right direction and help it avoid potential pitfalls.

Step 4. Identify the expected benefits and success measures

The final step is to ensure clarity around the expected benefits. Since each customization is in effect an experiment, the benefits and associated success measures will ensure that we can clearly decide whether the customization helped in the way we expected. Clearly defined benefits also help with prioritization and communication.

Creating an implementation plan for customizing SAFe

Inside an organization, SAFe may be utilized by hundreds, thousands, 10,000s, or even more individuals. As such, any customization to what is formally described in the Framework needs to be managed carefully.

Prioritizing the Customizations

The customization blueprint may contain several changes that the organization wishes to make at any one time. Considering where the organization is in its transformation journey can help with prioritization.

Communicating the Change

In terms of communicating the changes, each organization will need to decide on the best method based on the communication channels they have available and the scale of the customization. Some specific details should be included in every circumstance:

The Why Behind the Customization: Change for the sake of change rarely succeeds. Ensure that the communication clearly includes the reason for the change and links the customization back to the original need.
Details of the Customization: Be specific about the details of the customization. What is changing, and how will this change be represented in tools, reports, and other visible artifacts?
Scope of the Customization: There are often multiple SAFe implementations inside a single organization. The customization may not apply to all of them.
Optional vs Mandatory: Some customizations need to be implemented by everyone within the affected scope for it to be meaningful. Some customizations are optional.
Timelines for Change: It is important to determine the timeline by which each change is expected to be completed.

Implementing the Change

Although some customizations seem, at least on the surface, to be obvious changes, any adjustments to a system of work can have unintended consequences. Four considerations can help:

Take a Hypothesis-Driven Approach: Because the impact of the change is often uncertain, an experimental mindset is ideal. Identify the smallest amount of change needed to validate or invalidate quickly.
Educate People on the Change: Individuals such as Lean-Agile Center of Excellence (LACE) members, (A)SPCs, RTEs, and Scrum Masters are all well placed to help inform people about the change.
Monitor against the desired outcomes: The desired outcomes behind each customization become the measures against which the change is evaluated. Be mindful that change can take time before improvements can be seen.
Have a plan for rolling back or rolling out: Sometimes the next step will be to scale the customization across the organization, but sometimes it will be to roll back the changes and start again with another approach.
