This next step in the Implementation Roadmap is critical to realizing the full benefits of SAFe. Confidence earned with the first ART opens a significant business opportunity to launch more ARTs and value streams throughout the organization to serve customer needs and attain market objectives.

Details

As the Implementation Roadmap picture illustrates and experience has shown, the first PI Planning event, ART launch, and PI deliverables provide initial, measurable, and substantial business benefits. Within this first ART, business and development are now aligned to a shared vision and mission; everyone has agreed to a new way of working and has adopted a standard method, language, cadence, and synchronized events; new roles and responsibilities are established; a new level of employee engagement arises as teams take responsibility for planning their future.

In addition, the change leaders have the practical knowledge and experience they need to launch more ARTs. In this next critical move, the enterprise can realize an even greater return on its investment: faster time-to-market, higher quality, higher productivity, and increased employee engagement.

Launch More ARTs within a Value Stream

Building on the success of the first ART, SPCs and the LACE follow the same steps to launch more ARTs:

Prepare for ART launch
Train teams and launch the ART
Coach ART execution

Launching these subsequent ARTs requires the same attention and care as the first ART. Doing so provides each new set of people the same high-quality opportunity to learn and plants Lean-Agile practices and mindset into the organization's culture.

Apply Large Solutions Trains, Roles, and Practices as Needed

As described in Organize Around Value, some value streams can be realized by a single ART. They already have the people, resources, and cross-functional skills needed to Release on Demand without additional coordination, integration with other ARTs, or added governance. However, more significant value streams require hundreds or thousands of practitioners and may be subject to significant regulatory and compliance constraints.

Figure 1. SAFe Large Solution Configuration

Examples of circumstances where the enterprise solution delivery configuration applies include:

Large consumer solutions that host complex user journeys and experiences across multiple products and lines of business
Cyber-physical systems that require a broad range of engineering disciplines and utilize hardware and other long lead-time items
High assurance systems of any scope that are subject to significant safety and compliance requirements

In these cases, the next stage of the rollout will need to establish additional practices and roles:

Establishing roles – Fill the three large solution roles—Solution Train Engineer (STE), Solution Management, and Solution Architects, creating leadership and alignment across ARTs and critical stakeholders.
Establishing Solution Intent – Determine the responsibilities, process, and tools to define and maintain Solution Intent.
Establishing a large solution Vision, Roadmap, and Metrics – Many elements of the spanning palette may be required for large value streams.
Introducing the Solution Train Backlog – Larger value streams benefit from using the capabilities backlog item.
Implementing large solution events – These events are required to prepare for individual ART PI planning, coordinate Solution Train PI Objective delivery and dependencies, and demonstrate the whole solution to stakeholders.
Integrating suppliers – Large value streams typically have internal and external strategic Suppliers.

Lessons Learned and Guidance for Large-Scale SAFe Transformations

Northwestern Mutual Case Study – An Early and Effective Model

Northwestern Mutual was one of the first large-scale SAFe transformations. Top among Northwestern Mutual's innovations is the Big Visible Information Radiator (BVIR) which was used to visualize the flow of ARTs from identification to formation to launch and sustainment.

Figure 3. Northwestern Mutual's SAFe Transformation BVIR

Based on the success of the rollout and the interesting train metaphors Northwestern Mutual applied, the essence of this model was captured in the form of the 'SAFe Implementation Railway', shown in Figure 4.

Figure 4. The SAFe Implementation Railway

The railway is a Kanban system containing four major states:

Funnel – this state allows stakeholders to volunteer their value streams for transformation. Value streams are pulled to the next state when they have completed all preparation required to be ready for a successful launch.
Transformation Backlog – this is the ready-to-do state. Value streams in this state are continually prioritized for transformation based on support and readiness.
The Tracks – Within this state, multiple stations represent the work to identify, prepare, launch, operate, and transition the new ARTs to the new way of working.
Sustain and Improve – After an ART completes its journey on the track, including several successful PIs and evidence of successful Inspect and Adapt sessions, it is moved to the sustain and improve state.

Practical Considerations for Large-Scale Transformations

Balance Change Capacity – Demand for SAFe transformation grows with successful ART launches. Successfully pacing change requires balancing many factors, such as business priorities, risk, customer needs, and practical constraints.

Engage the Guiding Coalition – The guiding coalition is vital in maintaining momentum. More than ever, this coalition of leaders ensures that the vision is clear and communicated often.

Create Additional Lean-Agile Change Agents – Each new ART in the transformation Kanban requires SPCs to prepare, train, launch, and coach the ART.

Plan for Operational Activities – Additional ARTs and value streams require training, logistics, and communications.

Remediate Legacy Processes – Balancing the expansion of Lean-Agile with the need for changing legacy processes is crucial to sustained change.
