Business Owners

It is hard to imagine a more stupid or more dangerous way of making decisions than by putting those decisions in the hands of people who pay no price for being wrong.

– Thomas Sowell [1]

Definition: Business Owners (BOs) are key ART stakeholders who have the primary business and technical responsibility for return on investment (ROI), governance, and compliance.

Summary

Business Owners are key ART stakeholders with the business and technical responsibility for return on investment (ROI), governance, and compliance. They help to ensure a product or solution will meet the intended purpose in a manner aligned with the business needs and actively participate in Agile Release Train (ART) events.

What is a Business Owner in SAFe?

The role of the Business Owner is critical in SAFe. They provide crucial context and decision-making that guide the development process. Their involvement ensures that the solutions created are aligned with strategic business goals, meet customer needs, and deliver value. Business Owners are responsible for enabling decisions about risk and trade-offs. Their involvement helps to ensure that these decisions are made with a clear understanding of business implications.

Understanding the responsibilities and skills of current leaders is the best way to identify who will adopt the Business Owner's role in SAFe. Questions to identify Business Owners include:

Who is ultimately responsible for business outcomes?
Who can steer this ART to develop the right solutions?
Who can speak to the technical competence of the solution now and in the future?
Who should participate in planning, help eliminate impediments, and speak on behalf of development, the business, and the customer?
Who can approve and defend a set of PI plans, knowing they will never satisfy everyone?
Who can help ARTs coordinate efforts with other departments and organizations, spanning organizational boundaries?

While there is no rule on which individuals fulfill the role of Business Owner, the following are commonly seen: General or line of business managers, Product or Solution Management, Enterprise Architects, C-level executives, Operations executives, Senior engineering leaders, or Customers (for custom solutions).

Start with the smallest possible set of Business Owners for an ART and add members if it becomes clear that someone with the necessary accountability, skill, knowledge, or expertise is missing. Four to six Business Owners is fairly typical. A mix of business-oriented and technical people is recommended. It's reasonable to expect the Business Owners to change as the focus of work on the ART changes.

Leading by Example

As Lean-Agile leaders, it's vital that Business Owners practice and model the behaviors and attitudes of business agility. This way, they inspire others to follow their example. To do this, Business Owners:

Serve as an example of the new behaviors – Business Owners follow Lean-Agile principles and practices to demonstrate the new norms of behavior for the ART and others to follow. They also address shortcomings in SAFe knowledge and experience.
Communicate the vision for SAFe adoption – Frequently communicate the business need, urgency, and vision for change. Business Owners participate in developing the SAFe implementation plan, prioritizing the transformation backlog, and establishing metrics for tracking the progress of one or more ARTs.
Actively engage with the Lean-Agile Center of Excellence (LACE) – Address problems teams cannot resolve. Such issues are often beyond the LACE's control. For example, they may require facility changes, funding, hiring, and purchasing authority.
Address the concerns of people who resist change – Show empathy and compassion, addressing people's fears and worries. Then, resolve problems quickly to help overcome the resistance that may block the change. Share a clear mission and vision to inspire those who resist or fear change. Help everyone understand the new way of working and how it will benefit them, their team members, and the organization as a whole.
Act as change agents – Sincerely communicate belief in the vision for future change. When people see their leaders acting in ways that support change, they are more likely to become advocates for it and adopt new behaviors quickly. These leaders adapt roles, practices, and processes to help the organization and the ART.

A lack of psychological safety at work can have significant consequences. The organization is vulnerable when people feel uncomfortable discussing what is not working. After all, no one can fix a secret. Fear often leads to disengaged employees and missed opportunities to leverage their talent and dedication. People need to feel comfortable speaking up and asking naive questions. They need to be able to experiment with new ways of working and disagree with changes to create and implement ideas that make a difference.

Engaged employees adopt the organization's vision, values, and purpose. They become passionate contributors, innovative problem solvers, and dependable colleagues.

Engaging in Lean Portfolio Management

Although the Portfolio Leadership Team is ultimately responsible for the business outcomes associated with Lean Portfolio Management, Business Owners are often involved in the process. Some may serve as LPM executives, but most participate to some extent in activities such as:

Strategy and Investment Funding – They ensure that value streams are aligned and funded to create and maintain the products and solutions that deliver on business objectives.
Agile Portfolio Operations – Business Owners are responsible for helping value streams and ARTs get the right thing out the door to their customers. They may also directly or indirectly support the LACE and foster communities of practice (CoPs) within their domain of concern.
Lean Governance – Business Owners are engaged in prioritizing the ART backlog and making trade-off decisions to maximize economic outcomes. They also offer input on spending, audit, compliance, forecasting expenses, and measurement.
Occasionally serve as Epic Owners – Business Owners may serve as Epic Owners for initiatives that benefit from their domain knowledge, experience, and authority.
Lean Budgeting – Business Owners actively assist Portfolio Leadership in allocating the portfolio budget to the value streams.

Aligning Priorities & PI Planning

Business Owners are responsible for understanding and refining the strategic themes that influence what ARTs build. They know the organization, the portfolio, and the value stream context, and they help manage the product and solution vision and roadmap. The continuous involvement of Business Owners during the PI serves as a critical guardrail for the ART's budget.

The period before PI planning is busy for Business Owners. Their responsibilities include:

Provide input to backlog refinement – Participate in activities to align the ART backlog with the portfolio's strategic themes
Ensure that business objectives are understood – Ensure that the train's stakeholders agree to the business objectives. This includes the Release Train Engineer (RTE), Product Management, System Architects, and other Business Owners.
Prepare to communicate the business context – Prepare to describe the business's current state, the portfolio vision, and their perspective on whether existing products and solutions address current customer needs

PI Planning
ART and Solution Train Backlog

The importance of the Business Owner's role during PI planning cannot be overstated. Activities include:

Present the business context and vision – Share the business context during the defined PI planning time. This context may include the state of the business, market rhythms, milestones, and significant external dependencies.
Engage during critical ART PI planning activities – Participate in draft plan reviews, assign business value to team PI objectives, and approve final plans.
Review draft and final plans – Understand the bigger picture and determine if the team's PI objectives fulfill achieve business outcomes when taken together. Business Owners ask powerful questions and ensure alignment.
Watch for significant external commitments and dependencies – Foster the management of dependencies and support their reduction or elimination.
Actively circulate during planning – Communicate business priorities to the teams and maintain agreement and alignment among the stakeholders regarding the train's key objectives.
Participate in management review and problem-solving – Business Owners are critical stakeholders in this problem-solving meeting. They review and adjust the scope, remedy concerns, and compromise as necessary.
Assign Business Value to final PI Objectives – Business Owners score the team's PI objectives with planned Business Value from 1 (lowest) to 10 (highest). The simplest way to start is to assign a 10 to the highest individual objectives, typically 'fixed' commitments or must-have items, and then scale down from there. Giving too many PI objectives a 10 in a single Agile Team indicates a lack of clear priorities. This can result in the team having no business priorities. The Business Owner's knowledge and experience of the customer, market potential, and business priorities should be clearly shared through this discussion with each Agile Team.

They also discuss with the technical experts who know how certain architectural issues can help the team work faster and deliver more value. Properly valuing enabler work, for example, motivates the team and shows the business owners' commitment to resolving critical technical challenges.

When business owners assign business value during PI planning, it leads to vital conversations. This helps create personal connections between Agile teams and Business Owners. Together, they address shared concerns that require teamwork and gain a better understanding of business goals and their importance.

Realizing Business Outcomes

The Business Owner's job is not complete when PI planning is done. They have a role in helping ensure the ART executes successfully, which includes:

Maintain alignment – Actively maintain alignment between the business and development as priorities and scope inevitably change.
Help validate the definition of MVPs – Guide pivot-or-persevere decisions based on the delivery of Minimum Viable Products (MVPs).
Attend the System Demos – Engage in system demos to understand progress and provide feedback.
Attend Agile Team events – Attend Iteration planning, review, and retrospectives as needed.
Actively address impediments – Help resolve impediments that escalate beyond the authority of the train's leaders and stakeholders.
Participate in release management – Participate in release governance and determine when products and solutions are released. Specifically, focus on scope, quality, and market considerations.

Sponsoring Relentless Improvement

Inspect and Adapt (I&A) is a cadence-based event that allows the entire ART to reflect on progress and identify its impediments. During the event, Business Owners assess the value achieved versus the plan and participate in the I&A's problem-solving workshop. Moreover, Business Owners are Lean-Agile Leaders who:

Continually focus on eliminating waste and delays – Foster adopting Principle #6, Make value flow without interruption. That includes applying the eight flow accelerators, and the six flow measurements.
Eliminate demotivating policies and procedures – Participate in the I&A's problem-solving workshop to identify and eliminate systemic issues, policies, and processes that are not aligned with the Lean-Agile mindset and outside the scope of the ART's control.
Inspire and motivate others – Frequently communicate why change is needed in ways that inspire, motivate, and encourage buy-in with a sense of urgency.
Create a culture that values relentless improvement – Model the right behaviors to help transform a negative, power-oriented, and bureaucratic workplace (rule-oriented) into a positive, performance-oriented culture. This is essential for the Lean-Agile mindset to flourish.
Provide the time and space for teams to innovate – Foster the use of IP iterations to provide regular, cadence-based opportunities for teams to work on innovation, improvement activities, and learning. These activities are often difficult to fit into a continuous, incremental value-delivery pattern.
Help drive investment in the continuous delivery pipeline (CDP) – Support process and infrastructure enhancements to the CDP to improve the ART's responsiveness and the quality of its products and solutions.

It cannot be emphasized enough that the active participation of business owners is critical to the SAFe organization.

Key Takeaways
Business Owners have business and technical accountability for the return on investment (ROI), governance, and compliance.
The Business Owner enables the self-managing, self-organizing Agile Teams and ARTs essential to the success of SAFe.
To help organizations adopt SAFe, Business Owners practice and model the behaviors of Lean-Agile leadership.
