# SAFe Core Values and Principles

SAFe is grounded in four core values and ten immutable Lean-Agile principles. These are not aspirational ideals — they are the behavioral and economic foundations that make SAFe practices work. Without them, the practices will inevitably fail to deliver the intended business results.

## The Four Core Values

SAFe 6.0 core values: **alignment**, **transparency**, **respect for people**, and **relentless improvement**.

### Alignment

Misaligned organizations develop serious problems — hard to steer, slow to respond to change. In SAFe, many decisions are decentralized (see Principle #9), but without alignment those decisions pull in different directions. Clear alignment flows from the top of the enterprise through every level.

How to create and maintain alignment:
- **Communicate vision, mission, and strategy** — keep higher aims constantly present (e.g., Business Owner briefings during PI Planning)
- **Connect strategy to execution** — Strategic Themes translate business strategy into tangible guidance for portfolios, budgets, and epics
- **Common language** — SAFe roles, processes, events, and artifacts form a shared vocabulary
- **Regular reinforcement** — iteration planning, PI planning, ART syncs, portfolio syncs, and shared backlogs and boards keep alignment visible
- **Customer understanding** — Continuous Exploration, customer centricity, and Design Thinking keep backlogs aligned to real customer needs

### Transparency

Without openness, decision-making is based on speculation. Without trust, transparency cannot exist. Key practices:
- Create a trust-based environment — make and keep commitments, relinquish control
- Communicate directly, openly, honestly ("the facts are friendly")
- Turn mistakes into learning moments — psychological safety to surface errors without blame
- Visualize all work — backlogs, Kanban boards, ART boards, PI objectives, collaboration tools
- Provide ready access to information — make knowledge frictionless to find

### Respect for People

Lean and Agile methods are human-centric. When treated with respect, people are unleashed to evolve practices and contribute creativity. Disrespect creates toxic environments, poor performance, and high attrition.

Practices for cultivating respect:
- Hold precious what it is to be human — Toyota's "respect for people" means fostering creativity while honoring mutual trust
- Value diversity of people and opinions — build diverse organizations and genuinely listen to different perspectives
- Grow people through coaching and mentoring — develop people to make better products
- Treat internal stakeholders as customers too
- Build long-term supplier partnerships based on mutual benefit — "win-win" contracts, not bullying or price pressure

### Relentless Improvement

Striving for perfection, even though it's unattainable, leads to continuous improvement. Improvement requires learning through small iterative experiments (Plan-Do-Check-Adjust cycles).

Practices for relentless improvement:
- Create a constant sense of urgency — improvement is essential for survival, not a nice-to-have
- Build a problem-solving culture — PDCA cycles at all scales
- Reflect and adapt frequently — pause from the backlog to identify and address shortcomings
- Let facts guide improvements — empirical evidence over opinion
- Provide time and space for innovation — especially in the IP Iteration

## The Ten Lean-Agile Principles

SAFe synthesizes Agile, Lean product development, systems thinking, and DevOps into ten foundational principles. When practices fall short, these principles guide teams back toward "shortest sustainable lead time, with best quality and value to people and society."

### Principle #1 — Take an Economic View

Economics should inform and drive decisions at all levels, from Portfolio to Agile Teams. Poor economics is one of the most common causes of failed solutions.

Two practices for optimum economic outcomes:
- **Deliver early and often** — Lean-Agile delivers value much earlier than waterfall. Value accumulates from the moment of delivery; earlier arrival = more value accrued. Even an MVP delivered early may be worth more than a full-featured product delivered late.
- **Apply a comprehensive economic framework** — every SAFe portfolio needs decision guidelines that align everyone with financial objectives:
  - Operate within Lean Budgets and Guardrails
  - Understand solution economic trade-offs (development cost, lead time, product cost, value, risk)
  - Leverage suppliers strategically (transactional vs. long-term partnership)
  - Sequence jobs with WSJF (Weighted Shortest Job First) to minimize Cost of Delay

### Principle #2 — Apply Systems Thinking

Three aspects of systems thinking in SAFe:

1. **The solution is a system** — optimize the whole, not components; attend to interfaces and dependencies; the system can only evolve as fast as its slowest integration point
2. **The enterprise building the system is also a system** — avoid local optimization; eliminate functional silos; cross-functional ARTs and Solution Trains accelerate flow
3. **Optimize the full development value stream** — value stream mapping reveals that value-added steps are only a small fraction of total time; focus relentlessly on reducing wait time between steps

Only management can change the system. Lean-Agile Leaders must exhibit systems thinking, eliminate roadblocks, and take a long-term view.

### Principle #3 — Assume Variability; Preserve Options

Traditional stage-gated development tries to eliminate variability early by choosing one design option upfront. This leads to "false positive feasibility" — teams believe they're on track, but problems are hidden and surface late.

Better approach — **Set-Based Design (SBD)**: maintain multiple design options longer, continuously evaluate technical and economic trade-offs, eliminate weaker options using empirical data from integration points, and converge on a final design based on actual knowledge rather than early assumptions. This produces better technical and economic outcomes.

### Principle #4 — Build Incrementally with Fast, Integrated Learning Cycles

Traditional phase-gated development accumulates value only at the end. Fast, integrated cycles apply the PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Adjust) learning loop continuously.

Key insights:
- Shorter PDCA cycles = faster learning; reduce the cost of risk-taking by truncating unsuccessful paths quickly
- Integration points control product development — they are leverage points for improving the system
- Slipping integration points is the earliest warning that a project is in trouble
- Integration points serve dual purpose: validate technical feasibility AND test market/user fitness (MVPs, prototypes)

### Principle #5 — Base Milestones on Objective Evaluation of Working Systems

Phase-gate milestones fail because they centralize decisions in silos, force premature design choices, and create large-batch handoffs. There is no correlation between passing phase gates on time and project success.

SAFe alternative: Integration-based milestones at every PI. The PI System Demo provides:
- **Progress metrics** — is the ART on track for its mission?
- **Product metrics** — how is the solution performing in the market?
- **Process metrics** — what improvement items are in flight?

Stakeholders can frequently assess and decide whether to continue investing — financial, technical, and fitness-for-purpose governance throughout the life cycle.

### Principle #6 — Make Value Flow Without Interruptions

Flow occurs when there is smooth, linear, fast movement of work through the value stream. All flow systems share eight properties: WIP, bottlenecks, handoffs, feedback, batch, queue, workers, and policies.

**Eight Flow Accelerators:**
1. **Visualize and Limit WIP** — Kanban boards expose overload; WIP limits match demand to capacity
2. **Address Bottlenecks** — the throughput of the entire system is limited by the dominant constraint (Theory of Constraints)
3. **Minimize Handoffs and Dependencies** — cross-functional teams reduce wait states and rework from imperfect knowledge transfer
4. **Get Faster Feedback** — PDCA cycles, customer engagement, CI/CD pipeline, small work items, stop-the-line quality
5. **Work in Smaller Batches** — smaller batches move faster, accumulate less variability, and produce faster feedback
6. **Reduce Queue Length** — long queues delay response to customer needs; Little's Law: wait time = queue length ÷ processing rate
7. **Optimize Time 'In the Zone'** — protect uninterrupted focus time for knowledge workers; flow state = higher creativity and productivity
8. **Remediate Legacy Policies** — proactively eliminate phase gates, change control boards, and other practices that were designed for waterfall

**Six Flow Metrics** (from Measure and Grow): Flow Distribution, Flow Velocity, Flow Time, Flow Load, Flow Efficiency, Flow Predictability.

### Principle #7 — Apply Cadence, Synchronize with Cross-Domain Planning

**Cadence** — a rhythmic, predictable pattern of events that makes routine everything that can be routine, freeing teams to focus on innovation. PI intervals limit variance to one timebox; IP Iteration provides schedule margin.

**Synchronization** — multiple perspectives understood, resolved, and integrated at the same time. System Demos synchronize ART-level work; PI Planning synchronizes cross-domain planning.

Together, cadence and synchronization create a "safety zone" where enough uncertainty enables innovation while business stakeholders retain sufficient confidence to plan and commit. Infrequent planning allows deviations to accumulate; cadence-based planning catches and corrects variances small and cheaply.

### Principle #8 — Unlock the Intrinsic Motivation of Knowledge Workers

Traditional extrinsic incentives (reward/punish) work for routine, algorithmic tasks. Knowledge work is heuristic — novel, creative, exploratory. Introducing extrinsic rewards to heuristic work *degrades* performance.

Intrinsic motivation requires three "psychological nutrients":
- **Autonomy** — self-direction; lead with objectives (why/what), delegate the how; decentralize decisions; allocate exploratory time
- **Mastery** — the drive to continually improve; challenging activities, learning from errors, Built-in Quality practices, time "in the zone"
- **Purpose** — connecting to the why; customer connection, mission/vision sharing, bridge work to outcomes, team-based goal-setting

Compensation should connect to collective purpose, use appreciation over if-then incentives, foster collaboration over internal competition, and use continuous feedback over annual reviews.

### Principle #9 — Decentralize Decision-Making

Centralized decision-making creates queues, blocks progress, and yields suboptimal outcomes because central decision-makers lack local context. Decentralizing reduces delays, improves flow, enables faster feedback, and fosters innovation and engagement.

Prerequisites for effective decentralization:
- **Technical competence** — control without competence is chaos
- **Organizational clarity** — everyone understands values, purpose, mission, and their role; enabled by Strategic Themes (OKRs), Solution Vision, Roadmap, PI Planning, Backlogs

**Anatomy of a decision**: consider Information, Authority, Competence, Clarity, and Impact. Then choose: escalate, defer to Last Responsible Moment, take the decision, or delegate.

**When to centralize vs. decentralize**: Centralize when Frequency is low, Time-criticality is low, Economies of scale are high. Decentralize otherwise.

### Principle #10 — Organize Around Value

Traditional functional hierarchies optimize efficiency at the cost of speed and adaptability. The digital age demands organizing around value delivery, not functional expertise.

SAFe's dual operating system:
- **Network** — optimized for speed and adaptability; entrepreneurial, organized around value streams
- **Hierarchy** — optimized for efficiency and stability; HR, finance, governance, operations

Business Agility requires maintaining both. When market and customer demands change, the enterprise must quickly reorganize around the new value flow. ARTs and Solution Trains are the primary mechanism for value-stream alignment.

## Leadership Is Required

Consistently applying core values requires active support from [[SAFe-leadership-and-culture]]. Leaders must exemplify the core values, model Lean-Agile mindset, and create a persistent, values-based culture. The principles provide the underlying rationale for all SAFe practices — when practices fall short, return to the principles.
