# SAFe Leadership and Culture

The Leadership and Culture (L&C) Discipline explains how leaders build a positive, high-performing culture where people and teams can achieve their best. It is the essential connective tissue for a successful SAFe transformation. Without active, committed Lean-Agile leadership at all levels, SAFe implementations stall, produce thin veneers of new vocabulary over unchanged processes, and fail to deliver lasting business results.

The ultimate goal is to create a **Lean-Agile Organization** that competes and thrives by responding quickly to market changes with innovative products and services.

## Business Agility: Why It Matters

Business Agility is the ability to compete and thrive in the digital age by quickly responding to market changes and emerging opportunities with innovative, digitally-enabled business solutions.

The challenge: Most organizational structures, management practices, and cultures were built over a century ago for control and stability, not innovation and speed. In the Age of Software and Digital, digital disruption is the norm, and the organizations that cannot adapt face decline.

**Dual Operating System** (Kotter): Successful enterprises need both:
- **Hierarchy** — optimized for efficiency, stability, HR, finance, governance, and operations
- **Adaptive Network** — optimized for speed, innovation, and customer focus; organized around value streams rather than functional silos

SAFe is the second operating system. It creates ARTs (Development Value Streams) that function as the entrepreneurial network, operating within and alongside the existing hierarchy. This allows organizations to focus on customers, products, and innovation without dismantling existing structures.

When the hierarchy overwhelms the network:
- The transformation stalls after initial wins
- Fixed mindsets preserve old ways of working
- New silos emerge with territorial behaviors
- Leaders don't provide development or incentives for permanent change
- Strategy stays disconnected from execution

## Becoming Lean-Agile Leaders

Leaders are the primary drivers of cultural change. Their mindset and behaviors shape the organization's culture — whether positive or negative. The most effective change technique is for leaders to model the behaviors and mindsets they want to see in others.

**Why Lean-Agile Leaders are essential:**
- Only leaders have the authority to change the systems that govern how work is performed
- Only leaders can create the environment for high-performing Agile teams to flourish
- Leadership cannot be delegated: "Not enough that management commit themselves to quality and productivity, they must know what it is they must do." (Deming)

**Lean-Agile Leader attributes for the digital age (MIT research):**
- Insatiable learning — leaders must be voracious learners
- Authenticity — genuine engagement with transformation, not performative
- Emotional competence — self-awareness, empathy, psychological safety
- Courage — challenge status quo; enable others to challenge it too
- Growing others — develop people, build capability
- Decentralized decision-making — trust and empower knowledge workers

**Core Lean-Agile Mindset:**
- Embody SAFe's Four Core Values (alignment, transparency, respect for people, relentless improvement)
- Consistently apply the SAFe Ten Principles
- Study and teach Lean and Agile methods
- Lead with systems thinking — optimize the whole, not the parts

## Leading Change: Kotter's Eight Accelerators

SAFe's Implementation Roadmap closely follows Kotter's eight change accelerators:

1. **Create a sense of urgency** — Help others see the need for change and the importance of acting now; demonstrate strong personal advocacy
2. **Build a guiding coalition** — A committed group with the right leadership, credibility, and analytical skills; the LACE is a key part of this coalition
3. **Form a strategic vision** — Clear, compelling description of how things will be after SAFe adoption; Transformation OKRs articulate specific objectives
4. **Enlist a volunteer army** — Successful transformation requires broad-based participation; training is a start but knowledge alone is insufficient
5. **Enable action by removing barriers** — Identify impediments, discover root causes, develop solutions; leaders clear the path
6. **Generate short-term wins** — Create visible, unambiguous success early and often; faster flow, better quality, motivated teams, happy customers
7. **Sustain acceleration** — Avoid declaring victory too early; long-lived, well-funded LACE provides ongoing training and coaching
8. **Institute change** — Adjust processes, policies, and systems to anchor the new behaviors; Lean-Agile becomes "just the way we do things here"

Organizations that changed leadership culture as part of value delivery transformation **dominate their respective markets**. Organizations that focused on tools and ceremonies without cultural change produced only surface-level results.

## Continuous Learning Culture (CLC)

CLC describes a set of values and practices that encourage individuals and the enterprise to continually increase knowledge, competence, performance, and innovation. It is part of the SAFe Foundation and included in all SAFe configurations.

Three dimensions of CLC:
1. **Learning Organization** — Individuals and teams create, share, and apply new knowledge continuously; apply the Cynefin framework to understand context and choose appropriate practices
2. **Relentless Improvement** — Everyone at every level pursues continuous improvement; PDCA cycles at team, ART, and portfolio levels; Inspect & Adapt as a structural forcing function
3. **Culture of Innovation** — Psychological safety to experiment, fail safely, and share learnings; time for innovation (IP Iteration); Communities of Practice; hackathons

**Communities of Practice (CoPs)** — Groups of people with shared interests who collaborate and exchange knowledge across teams and ARTs. CoPs for Scrum Masters, Product Owners, architects, and other disciplines spread best practices and prevent siloed learning.

**Measure and Grow** approach: Data-driven decisions rather than opinions. Competency self-assessments at regular intervals identify improvement opportunities. Baseline the assessment at transformation start, measure progress continuously.

## Organizational Agility

Organizational Agility describes how Lean-thinking people and Agile Teams optimize business processes, evolve strategy with decisive commitments, and quickly adapt to capitalize on new opportunities.

Three dimensions:
1. **Lean-Thinking People and Agile Teams** — Everyone involved in solution delivery is trained in Lean and Agile; embraces values, principles, and practices
2. **Lean Business Operations** — Teams apply Lean principles to understand, map, and continuously improve business-supporting processes (not just technology delivery)
3. **Strategy Agility** — The enterprise can sense the market and quickly change strategy when necessary; portfolio and leadership capability to pivot

Organizational agility extends Lean-Agile beyond development teams to the entire enterprise: finance, HR, marketing, legal, security, compliance, and operations. All must work with the same mindset and principles.

| Leading Change | Rapid change is overwhelming; leaders lack mindset and skills to lead transformations |
| Transforming People Development | Workforce development model too slow and outdated; critical skills gaps |
| Operating Agile Management Teams | Management teams using traditional approaches; limiting true Agile transformation benefits |
| Developing Leaders | Leadership pipeline gap threatens ability to navigate complexity |
| Adapting and Innovating | Insufficient leaders with courage and agility to challenge status quo |
| Leading by Example | Transformation stalls when leaders fail to model needed mindsets and behaviors |
| Balancing the Dual OS | Misalignment between Agile ways and traditional hierarchy causing confusion and delays |

## The Guiding Coalition

A SAFe transformation requires a coalition of influential, respected, and knowledgeable individuals committed to leading and communicating the change. Three components:

1. **Executives and Leaders** — Primary drivers; commit authority, resources, and personal example
2. **SAFe Practice Consultants (SPCs)** — Trained change agents who facilitate transformation and provide coaching; formerly called SAFe Program Consultants
3. **Lean-Agile Center of Excellence (LACE)** — Small dedicated team coordinating change efforts; see [[SAFe-lean-portfolio-management]] for LACE detail
4. **Local Champions** — Individuals at all levels across departments who ensure new ways of working stick and become cultural norms

## Common Failure Patterns

Organizations that fail to develop Lean-Agile leaders experience:
- Transformation stalling after a few initial wins
- Fixed mindsets preserving old ways of working
- New silos with same territorial behaviors as before
- Leaders not providing development or incentives for permanent change
- Enterprise strategy disconnected from execution
- Thin veneer of Lean-Agile words masking unchanged processes and culture
