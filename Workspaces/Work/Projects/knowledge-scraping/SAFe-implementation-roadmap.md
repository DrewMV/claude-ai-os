# SAFe Implementation Roadmap

The SAFe Implementation Roadmap describes a strategy and an ordered set of activities for successfully implementing SAFe. It consists of 13 critical moves that take an enterprise from the initial imperative for change through full business agility. The roadmap is based on John Kotter's eight organizational change accelerators and provides a proven, sequential path for transformation.

**Core insight:** "Many leaders pride themselves on setting the high-level direction and staying out of the details. But big picture, hands off leadership isn't likely to work in a change situation, because the hardest part of change—the paralyzing part—is in the details. Any successful change requires a translation of ambiguous goals into concrete behaviors." (Chip & Dan Heath)

## The 13 Critical Moves

### Step 1: Reaching the Tipping Point

Before any transformation can begin, the organization must reach its tipping point — the point where the overriding organizational imperative is to change rather than resist it.

Two paths to the tipping point:
- **Burning platform** — The company is failing to compete; the existing way is obviously inadequate; urgency is self-evident
- **Visionary leadership** — In the absence of a burning platform, leadership proactively drives change by taking a stand for a better future state

A compelling vision for change provides three benefits:
- **Purpose** — Clarifies direction for the change
- **Motivation** — Moves people in the right direction
- **Alignment** — Starts the coordinated action necessary

The tipping point is often reached through shared leadership experiences such as the Leading SAFe training, which creates aligned understanding of what change is needed and why.

### Step 2: Train Lean-Agile Change Agents

Once the tipping point is reached, a "sufficiently powerful guiding coalition" is needed. This requires training Lean-Agile Change Agents as Certified SAFe Practice Consultants (SPCs).

An effective coalition requires:
- Leaders who can set vision, show the way, and remove impediments
- Practitioners, managers, and change agents who can implement specific process changes
- Sufficient organizational credibility to be taken seriously
- The expertise needed to make fast, intelligent decisions

SPCs are sourced internally and externally from: consulting partners, internal business/technology leaders, portfolio/program/project managers, and trusted individual contributors with organizational credibility.

### Step 3: Create a Lean-Agile Center of Excellence (LACE)

The LACE is a small, dedicated Agile team whose primary task is implementing and sustaining SAFe change. It is a critical component of the guiding coalition. See [[SAFe-lean-portfolio-management]] for detailed LACE responsibilities.

Key LACE characteristics: respected peers, motivated by helping others, pragmatic optimism.

### Step 4: Train Executives, Managers, and Leaders

Leaders and managers require specific training to lead Lean-Agile ways of working:
- **Leading SAFe** — For all leaders; covers Lean-Agile mindset, principles, and leading transformation
- **Leading in the Digital Age** — Specific leadership behaviors required for digital transformation success

Without trained leadership, implementations stall — fixed mindsets preserve old ways, new silos emerge with the same territorial behaviors, and transformation relies on heroic individuals rather than a broad volunteer army.

### Step 5: Lead in the Digital Age

Once trained, Lean-Agile leaders must actively model the behaviors and mindsets that enable the organization to follow their example. Kotter's eight accelerators guide this work:

1. Create a sense of urgency — demonstrate strong personal advocacy for SAFe
2. Build a guiding coalition — properly form, fund, and empower the LACE
3. Form a strategic vision — communicate a clear, compelling future state; use Transformation OKRs
4. Enlist a volunteer army — training is a start; buy-in requires experience and visible wins
5. Enable action by removing barriers — identify impediments, discover root causes, develop solutions
6. Generate short-term wins — showcase faster flow, better quality, motivated teams
7. Sustain acceleration — avoid declaring victory too early; fund the LACE for ongoing coaching
8. Institute change — adjust processes, policies, and systems to anchor the new behaviors

### Step 6: Organize Around Value

Identify the Development Value Streams and organize the ARTs that will deliver them. Key activities:
- Conduct Value Stream and ART Identification Workshop
- Define current-state value stream map
- Identify ARTs and their scope (products/solutions)
- Identify team structure within each ART
- Establish initial ART Backlogs and roadmaps

This step renamed from "Identify ARTs and Value Streams" in SAFe 6.0 to emphasize that the goal is organizing around value flow, not simply identifying structures.

### Step 7: Create the Implementation Plan

Develop a concrete plan for the implementation with:
- Timeline for ART launches
- Training schedules for all roles
- Identification of Business Owners, RTEs, and ART leadership
- Infrastructure and tooling requirements
- Communication plan

The implementation plan is itself managed as an Agile backlog — prioritized, iterative, and visible.

### Step 8: Prepare for ART Launch

ART launch preparation is critical to a successful PI 1. Activities:
- **Train Product Managers, Product Owners, and other ART stakeholders**
- Establish ART Backlog — prioritized feature list ready for PI Planning
- Define Solution Vision and initial Roadmap
- Identify System Architects and establish architectural vision
- Prepare logistics for PI Planning (physical or virtual)
- Pre-socialize features with teams before PI Planning

### Step 9: Train Teams and Launch the ART

The most powerful event in the launch sequence is PI Planning. Train teams immediately before or just after PI Planning:
- All teams receive training (typically SAFe for Teams)
- Teams begin PI Planning for the first time — face-to-face if possible
- Teams identify iteration plans, PI Objectives, dependencies, and risks
- Business Owners assign business value
- ART conducts confidence vote

A well-executed first PI Planning creates immediate energy, alignment, and shared sense of purpose.

### Step 10: Coach ART Execution

After launch, the ART needs sustained coaching to master new ways of working:
- RTEs coach teams through first iterations and events
- SPCs and LACE provide coaching at System Demos, Inspect & Adapt events
- Address process issues that emerge during execution
- Help teams improve flow metrics
- Support teams in building their Continuous Delivery Pipeline
- Coach proper application of PI Objectives and WSJF

### Step 11: Launch More ARTs and Value Streams

After the first ART succeeds and demonstrates wins:
- Apply lessons learned from the first ART launch
- Scale by launching additional ARTs across the portfolio
- Connect related ARTs into Solution Trains where appropriate
- Extend the LACE to support growing transformation scope

Short-term wins from early ARTs provide the momentum and credibility needed to drive broader adoption.

### Step 12: Enhance the Portfolio

Activate Lean Portfolio Management (LPM) to connect the SAFe implementation to strategic decision-making:
- Establish Portfolio Leadership team and governance model
- Implement Portfolio Kanban for epic management
- Move from project-based to value stream funding (Lean Budgets)
- Launch Participatory Budgeting (Strategic Investment Planning)
- Establish Value Management Office (VMO)
- Measure portfolio-level outcomes, flow, and competency

This step renamed from "Extend to the Portfolio" in SAFe 6.0.

### Step 13: Accelerate

The SAFe transformation is never complete — it's a continuous journey toward business agility. Acceleration activities:
- Apply Measure and Grow assessments to identify next improvement areas
- Launch Communities of Practice across the enterprise
- Build toward full business agility across all functions (marketing, finance, HR, legal, operations)
- Continue extending Lean-Agile practices beyond development teams
- Maintain the LACE for ongoing coaching and improvement
- Apply SAFe to AI, Big Data, and Cloud integration

## Customizing SAFe

**Definition:** Customizing SAFe is when new elements are added or default definitions are modified. This is distinct from **configuring SAFe** (choosing standard options: ART organization, team composition, PI length, role names, etc.).

### Common Reasons to Customize

- Industry-specific regulatory requirements or operational models
- Organizational size and existing structural constraints
- Technological landscape (especially AI integration)
- Existing culture and language
- Specific business goals requiring tailored approaches
- Continuous learning — as SAFe knowledge grows, refinements emerge

### Four Customization Guardrails

1. **Enhance agility, RATHER THAN compromise the essence of SAFe** — Does the customization contradict the Lean-Agile Mindset, Core Values, or Principles? If so, extreme caution is warranted.
2. **When it is the right solution, RATHER THAN the easiest thing to do** — Customization is an advanced practice; use only when it's the best option, not the easiest escape.
3. **Amplify outcomes, RATHER THAN maintain the status quo** — Customizations should accelerate outcomes, not preserve existing ways of working.
4. **Optimize systemically, RATHER THAN solve local problems** — SAFe is an integrated system; verify that changes don't break other elements or introduce inconsistencies.

### Customization Process

1. Identify the need (often surfaced through I&A, retrospectives, or SAFe assessments)
2. Define the proposed customization
3. Validate against the four guardrails
4. Identify expected benefits and success measures
5. Implement incrementally with a hypothesis-driven approach
6. Monitor against desired outcomes; be ready to roll back if ineffective

### Examples of Valid Customizations

- **Government:** Rename "Business Value" to "Mission Value"; "Customer" to "Public Servant"; specialized SAFe for Government Big Picture
- **Hardware organizations:** Shorter or longer iteration cycles to match hardware development rhythms
- **Highly regulated industries:** More formal compliance gates integrated into Definition of Done
- **Shorter PI cadence:** 4–6 week PIs for faster feedback and maximum responsiveness

## Common Failure Patterns in SAFe Implementation

Organizations that fail to transform successfully typically exhibit:
- Transformation stalls after a few initial wins
- Fixed mindsets preserve old ways of working
- New silos emerge with same territorial behaviors
- Leadership provides training but not ongoing development or incentive model changes
- The transformation relied on heroic individuals, not a broad volunteer army
- Enterprise strategy remained disconnected from execution
- Thin veneer of SAFe language masks unchanged processes and culture

Organizations that **change leadership culture** as an integral part of changing how they deliver value dominate their respective markets.
